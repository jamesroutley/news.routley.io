Doing More Customer Interviews Won't Make You Better At Them

Founders and product managers fail at customer development, even when they're consistently talking to users. But this is less from conducting too few interviews and more from a missing feedback loop. The art of posing the right question is counterintuitive and cognitively taxing, so founders can‚Äôt analyze their own interviewing performance in real time. A transcript-based AI coach that flags hard-fail interviewing errors restores that loop‚Äîraising ground truth per hour faster and cheaper than brute-forcing more interviews.

Customer development is commonly phrased as advice to simply "talk to users," but that undersells the difficulty of doing it right. It's actually the ethnography of demand, with many unintuitive pitfalls, and it requires deliberate practice to do well. Avoiding these pitfalls is difficult to do in the heat of an interview.

But AI coaches can bridge that gap between theory and practice by providing specific actionable feedback to your specific customer interviews. This feedback is the critically missing piece to improve the skill of inferring customer demand by improving the number of ground truths per hour.

What makes customer development so hard? It's hard in two ways: 1) People usually can't provide a clear articulation of their problem or a feasible solution. 2) The questions you ask can unintentionally incentivize people to lie to you.

This is unintuitive. Founders think customers would be properly incentivized to be truthful about their problems and their solutions. Why would you lie about your needs? Reality is, most people are too busy trying to achieve some other goal to take a step back to introspect and articulate the problem. Hence, they may not identify the core problem, nor the most important problem. They're also not aware of all the technological solutions they can bring to bear on a problem. But that doesn't stop them from articulating their problems as solutions, which can often be misleading.

First-time founders can be naive to the Heisenberg-like effects of asking for customer opinions like, "would you use this?" and "what do you think of my idea?" Social norms of politeness dictate you don't trash something the other person is clearly emotionally invested in. So unless a founder's asking for money, it's often easier to say "Sure, I'd use it!", "I think lots of people would find it useful," and "It sounds like a great idea!" than to deal with the social awkwardness of telling someone's idea doesn't resonate with you. It's akin to telling someone their baby is ugly.

As a result, you cannot just build the solutions people ask for. People are experts in their problems, not their solutions. You cannot ask people about their problems directly. They can misjudge the value of a problem or how pressing a problem is. And you definitely cannot tell them your idea and ask them what they think about it and whether they'd buy. They'd sooner lie to you than to tell you your idea is bad or that they don't need your product.

With those constraints, what can you even ask a potential customer in an interview? The only reliable topics you can ask potential customers are recent recollections of their concrete actions when they came across a problem and the context in which they found themselves when the problem arose. Everything else from why their need exists to the product solution must be inferred.

Hence, customer development is unintuitive and requires deliberate practice to overcome these common pitfalls when interviewing potential customers. It requires just a bit of post-interview introspection and analysis on what went right, and what could be improved, in the same way professional basketball players review game footage.

Unlike professional sports, the customer development feedback loop is missing. But why do we need this feedback loop in the first place?

You can't run meta-analysis while you're interviewing. When you're trying to infer demand, your working memory is already overloaded. The right questions are counterintuitive, so you're juggling two hard tasks at once: finding the next question that gets to ground truth while listening closely enough to follow the thread of what's being said. It's predictable that you'd miss the meta-level entirely, because all your attention is consumed by the conversation itself.

By the time the interview ends, the details have already faded. You‚Äôre left with a few half-formed notes and a hazy memory, hardly enough for real analysis. Without an external coach to close that loop, you have no reliable way to see what you did well or poorly. And without that feedback, improvement stalls ‚Äî founders and product managers keep repeating the same mistakes, unaware until it's too late and they've built the wrong thing. It‚Äôs easy to read what good interviews should look like; it‚Äôs much harder to recognize those principles in the middle of a conversation, when you‚Äôre juggling multiple cues and counterintuitive moves at once.

Why not just do more interviews? Wouldn't brute-forcing the number of interviews help founders and product managers get better without needing the feedback mechanism?

You might be reminded of the parable of the pottery class graded on the quantity of pottery they made improved faster than the other class graded on the quality of the pottery they made. Yet, the parable doesn't apply.

Clay gives perfect feedback; conversations don't. It's clear when clay doesn't hold a shape. It's not clear when words reflect ground truth. Finished pottery exists afterward; conversations are fleeting and ephemeral. Concrete evidence leads to higher fidelity feedback and analysis.

Hence, grinding on quantity only works when the medium gives perfect feedback. Customer interviews give noisy feedback: words are biased, memories are selective, and without transcripts at the very least, you're learning from low-fidelity notes. If your interviewing error rate stays high with leading questions, future hypotheticals, or failure to quantify budget or urgency, then doing more interviews mostly scales the noise. When you tamp down the error rate, the speed to ground truth increases, and that compounding effect takes over. The founder who learns to extract ground truth in a handful of interviews will outpace one who needs dozens just to see the same signal.

How do you leverage an AI coach in a customer development workflow? It's a straightforward two-step process. First, you convert the audio conversation of the interview into a transcript. Then, you use a prompt where the AI plays the role of a customer development coach to analyze the transcript and write an analysis of your performance.

Now that audio-to-text transcription works really well with the current crop of multi-modal LLMs, it's possible to get accurate transcripts of a conversation for cheap. The transcription can happen in real-time, on your own machine, so you don't even need to record the raw audio, or send it to remote servers. When I hop on a call for a customer interview, I'll ask the interviewee for permission to record the audio portion of the call to get a transcript. Nine out of ten people will agree to it. I think they appreciate being asked. I currently use Granola.ai, but MacWhisper or other audio transcript software will do. After the interview, I'll copy and paste the transcript into a markdown file named interview_transcript.md

Instead of taking notes that I'll never read again when reading a book on customer development, such as Jobs-to-be-done, the Mom Test, Sales Safari, or any number of resources written on customer development, I'll distill their principles into a ~2200 word prompt instead. The prompt has sections on the agent's objective, instructions, context and principles, and examples to help guide and guardrail the AI agent into doing the analysis correctly. This gets written into a file named cust_dev_coach.md

Is most of the benefit just having the transcript, and not the AI coach? It's true that a transcript makes an ephemeral conversation tangible. However, it takes a lot of effort to review a transcript post-interview. You'd have to relive the same conversation and manually map principle to practice. Most people don't do this because it's both taxing and time consuming. AI coaches lower this cost to almost nothing, while keeping the analysis specific and actionable.

Why not use a human coach? Customer development coaching doesn't seem to be a thing in Silicon Valley. Having lurked on Hacker News, Indie Hackers, and Bookface (YC's internal forum) for years, I've heard founders ask for recommendations for executive coaches, health and fitness coaches, and even couples therapy coaches, but never customer development coaches. Given customer development is often framed as just "talking to users," perhaps it's not a thing for the same reason ski instructors are more numerous than running instructors: running seems intuitive in a way that skiing doesn't.

But even if human coaching was available, it would be much more expensive. A human coach could cost on the order of thousands, whereas running a prompt would cost anywhere from under a dollar to $3. While human coaches could provide more value beyond pointing out mistakes, just avoiding mistakes will get you most of the benefit. As we've established earlier, a lowered error rate compounds.

There are additional benefits to using this workflow. By transcribing the audio into a transcript, you'll always have the raw information to perform additional analysis in the future, when your AI coach prompt improves or when there's another method of inferring customer demand that works better. And if all else fails, you can read the first-hand source material for insights you might have missed the first time.

So how do we know the AI coach is giving us good advice? Aren't LLMs prone to hallucination? The solution is to limit the AI coach to raising flags that are only hard-fail observable items. This includes:

  • Leading/loaded question detection
  • Future/hypothetical framing (‚ÄúWould you‚Ä¶‚Äù, ‚ÄúWould you pay‚Ä¶‚Äù)
  • Failure to ask budget/time/cost
  • Failure to ask for the last concrete event (‚ÄúTell me about the last time‚Ä¶‚Äù)
  • Interruption rate > X / min

It won't be giving judgements on "insightfulness", and it wouldn't be giving product advice.

Inexperienced LLM users expect AI to give exceptional performance with very little context. Like any human, the more relevant context you give them, the better they will do. Hence, detailed context and exposition on "what good looks like" in the prompt as gleaned from the source material on customer development would go a long way into guiding the LLM to flag these hard-fail items.

And if you do need to chase those edge cases where the AI agent fails, you can employ system evals. But that's rarely needed, because most of the mistakes founders make are common mistakes covered by the source material on customer development, rather than in rare edge cases.

Keep practicing interviewing, but it can now be deliberate, with feedback, due to the help from AI. Scaling up interviewing without closing the loop on improvement is wasted time.
