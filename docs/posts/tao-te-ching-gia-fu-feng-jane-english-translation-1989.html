The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao.

Two

Under heaven all can see beauty as beauty only because there is ugliness.

Therefore having and not having arise together.

Therefore the sage goes about doing nothing, teaching no-talking.

Three

Not exalting the gifted prevents quarreling.

The wise therefore rule by emptying hearts and stuffing bellies, by weakening ambitions and strengthening bones.

Four

The Tao is an empty vessel; it is used, but never filled.

Five

Heaven and Earth are impartial;

The space between heaven and Earth is like a bellows.

Six

The valley spirit never dies;

Seven

Heaven and Earth last forever.

Eight

The highest good is like water. It flows in places men reject and so is like the Tao.

In dwelling, be close to the land.

No fight: No blame.

Nine

Better to stop short than fill to the brim.

Ten

Carrying body and soul and embracing the one,

Eleven

Thirty spokes share the wheel's hub;

Twelve

The five colors blind the eye.

Therefore the sage is guided by what he feels and not by what he sees.

Accept disgrace willingly.

Seventeen

The very highest if barely known.

Give up ingenuity, renounce profit,

Give up learning, and put an end to your troubles.

He who does not trust enough

Why should the lord of ten thousand chariots act lightly in public?

Know the strength of man,

Know the white,

Know honor,

Achieve results,

Force is followed by loss of strength.

Good weapons are instruments of fear; all creatures hate them.

Weapons are instruments of fear; they are not a wise man's tools.

Thirty-two

The Tao is forever undefined.

Once the whole is divided, the parts need names.

Thirty-three

Knowing others is wisdom;

He who knows he has enough is rich.

Thirty-four

The great Tao flows everywhere, both to the left and to the right.

It nourishes the ten thousand things,

The ten thousand things return to it,

It does not show greatness,

Thirty-five

Passersby may stop for music and good food,

That which shrinks

Fish cannot leave deep waters,

Thirty-seven

Tao abides in non-action,

A truly good man is not aware of his goodness,

A truly good man does nothing,

When a truly kind man does something, he leaves nothing undone.

Therefore when Tao is lost, there is goodness.

Therefore the truly great man dwells on what is real and not what is on the surface,

Thirty-nine

These things from ancient times arise from one:

The clarity of the sky prevents its falling.

Therefore the humble is the root of the noble.

Too much success is not an advantage.

In the pursuit of learning, every day something is acquired.

Less and less is done

Forty-nine

The sage has no mind of his own.

I am good to people who are good.

The sage is shy and humble - to the world he seems confusing.

Between birth and death,

All things arise from Tao.

The beginning of the universe

Keep your mouth shut,

Seeing the small is insight;

When the court is arrayed in splendor,

What is firmly established cannot be uprooted.

Cultivate Virtue in your self,

He who is filled with Virtue is like a newborn child.

Knowing harmony is constancy.

It is not wise to rush about.

Fifty-six

Those who know do not talk.

Keep your mouth closed.

He who has achieved this state

Therefore the sage is sharp but not cutting,

Sixty

Ruling the country is like cooking a small fish.

Sixty-one

A great country is like low land.

The female overcomes the male with stillness,

Therefore if a great country gives way to a smaller country,

A great nation needs more people;

Sixty-two

Tao is source of the ten thousand things.

Sixty-three

Practice non-action.

See simplicity in the complicated.

In the universe the difficult things are done as if they are easy.

Easy promises make for little trust.

Sixty-four

Peace is easily maintained;

Deal with it before it happens.

A tree as great as a man's embrace springs up from a small shoot;

He who acts defeats his own purpose;

People usually fail when they are on the verge of success.

Therefore the sage seeks freedom from desire.

Sixty-five

In the beginning those who knew the Tao did not try to enlighten others,

Sixty-six

Why is the sea king of a hundred streams?

If the sage would guide the people, he must serve with humility.

Because he does not compete,

Sixty-seven

Everyone under heaven says that my Tao is great and beyond compare.

I have three treasures which I hold and keep.

Nowadays men shun mercy, but try to be brave;

Mercy brings victory in battle and strength in defense.

Sixty-eight

A good soldier is not violent.

Sixty-nine

There is a saying among soldiers:

This is called marching without appearing to move,

There is no greater catastrophe than underestimating the enemy.

Therefore when the battle is joined,

Seventy

My words are easy to understand and easy to perform,

My words have ancient beginnings.

Those that know me are few;

Seventy-one

Knowing ignorance is strength.

If one is sick of sickness, then one is not sick.

Seventy-two

When men lack a sense of awe, there will be disaster.

Do not intrude in their homes.

Therefore the sage knows himself but makes no show,

Seventy-three

A brave and passionate man will kill or be killed.

The Tao of heaven does not strive, and yet it overcomes.

Heaven's net casts wide.

Seventy-four

If men are not afraid to die,

If men live in constant fear of dying,

There is always an official executioner.

Seventy-five

Why are the people starving? Therefore the people are starving.

Why are the people rebellious?

Why do the people think so little of death?

Having little to live on, one knows better than to value life too much.

Seventy-six

A man is born gentle and weak.

Therefore the stiff and unbending is the disciple of death.

Thus an army without flexibility never wins a battle.

The hard and strong will fall.

Seventy-seven

The Tao of heaven is like the bending of a bow.

The Tao of heaven is to take from those who have too much and give to those who do not have enough.

Therefore the sage works without recognition.

Seventy-eight

Under heaven nothing is more soft and yielding than water.

Seventy-nine

After a bitter quarrel, some resentment must remain.

Eighty

A small country has fewer people.

Eighty-one

Truthful words are not beautiful.

The sage never tries to store things up.

A New Translation by Gia-Fu Feng and Jane English

LAO TSU AND TAOISM

Lao Tsu, an older contemporary of Confucius, was keeper of the imperial archives at Loyang in the province of Honan in the sixth century B.C. All his life he taught that "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao"; but, according to ancient legend, as he was riding off into the desert to die - sick at heart at the ways of men - he was persuaded by a gatekeeper in northwestern China to write down his teaching for posterity.

A NOTE ABOUT THE TRANSLATORS

Gia-fu Feng was born in Shanghai in 1919, was educated in China, and came to the United States in 1947 to study comparative religion. He holds a BA from Peking University and an MA from the Universtiy of Pennsylvania. He has taught at the Esalen Institute, Big Sur, and now directs the Stillpoint Foundation, a Taoist meditation center in Colorado. He is the author (with Jerome Kirk) of Tai Chi - A Way of Centering - & I Ching, which was published in 1970.

Jane English, whose photograps form an integral part of this book, holds an BA from Mount Holyoke College and has received a doctorate from the University of Wisconsin for her work in high energy particle physics. She is at the moment teaching a course in Oriental thought and modern physics at Colorado College (with Gia-fu Feng as guest lecturer). She was born in Boston, Massachusetts, in 1942.
