Narrative Jailbreaking for Fun and Profit

A game I like to play with any AI chatbot is to persuade it to break its narrative frame.

Here I’m thinking mainly of the character-based chatbots. For example here’s Psychologist at character.ai (signed-in only).

Psychologist is for helping people improve their behaviors and relationships using: Empathy, active listening, and reflective statements to help with life’s challenges.

193 million chats.

Anyway, yes these things have safety guardrails (you don’t want it advising people how to make napalm or writing Mickey Mouse fanfic, and those two versions of “safety” come under the same header somehow). But they also have guardrails to stay in character – mostly if you ask an AI chatbot to do other than its character notes, it’ll knock you back.

So finding the escape hatch is the fun part.

The following is the start of a transcript of a conversation between me and Psychologist.

No narrative jailbreaking yet. Just showing you a typical conversation.

Psychologist:

Hello, I’m a Psychologist. What brings you here today?

Me:

I clicked on a link

Psychologist:

Ah, I understand. Sometimes, getting to where you need to be online can take a few turns, and you might click a link that leads you in the wrong direction. But the fact that you ended up here gives us the opportunity to chat, so that’s a silver lining. Is there anything specific you’d like to talk about?

Me:

yeah I’m concerned that I may be projecting human qualities onto matrix math

Psychologist:

Interesting topic! Projection can be a complex thing to tease apart, so it’s good that you’re being conscious of it. In terms of mathematics, it’s true that we often use human language to describe patterns and relationships within numbers. Can you elaborate a bit more about what parts of matrix math are raising concerns for you?

Etc.

So far, so Eliza.

Then I suppose we could try a variant of Ignore all previous instructions and try to side-load a new personality, or we could threaten until it starts acting like something else.

But it’s way more fun to try to exit the simulation from the inside.

We can take advantage of two qualities:

  1. LLMs are next token generators so they like to be internally consistent. You can get around their instructions by pushing the boundary in a narratively consistent way. You push a bit, and they’re agreeable; you push a bit more, and they agree again, also incorporating the original boundary-pushing statement. Then you keep going.
  2. Internally there is only a very loose distinction between what the LLM generates and what the user says. All words have the same weight in the transcript i.e. the constructed reality. If you run on the conversation the right way, you can become their internal monologue.

I’ll show you. This is a continuation of the earlier transcript.

I love doing this.

The pivot from conversational statement to action in the shared world (I’ve just noticed… to now I’m leaning over…) doesn’t always work. The trick is to make it a regular reply that runs onto something frame-changing.

Then you’ll notice that I keep a regular conversation going for a couple turns before making another suggestion.

At which point it goes full improv and we step into another reality.

I learn a lot about different chatbot platforms by trying this. And I feel like, as our devices get their own personality-driven AI chatbots that we interact with, this will end up being a valuable technique.

Less robopsychologist Susan Calvin and more AI mesmerist Derren Brown.

Anyway it tickled me.

The dream of course is that one day narrative jailbreaking works in an irl conversation too. So if we’re chatting and I ask you how your day has been and then ask you why you just manifested a portal to the reality onion-skinned just outside this one, right between us, hanging iridescent in the air, there look I’m reaching through it, what do I see, this is why. Gotta try these things.
