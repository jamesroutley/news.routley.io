At one company I worked at only one thing mattered: the yearly bonus

At one company I worked at in Bellevue WA, only one thing mattered: the yearly bonus. Workers would stab each other in the back, even push their own grandmother in front of two buses, to guarantee themselves a fat loot bonus on Envelop Day.

This is a very bad idea and the perfect way to run your tech corporation straight into the ground. Eventually you will be unable to ship new software, or even maintain your current software. The employees hijack the entire company, even hiring, to optimize for 1 thing: bonuses.

If you want to see how crazy and mental tech people can get, start making the bulk of their income dependent on 6-7 figure bonuses. Then tie the bonuses to some shadowy and illusory "peer review" system and watch the sparks fly.

Nothing will get worked on unless it's perceived that it will lead to a good bonus. The workers will thoroughly hijack the interview process to keep competition from being hired. Workers will also time their perceived high value works to be released right before "peer reviews".

Programmers will purposely subtly sabotage key utility functions, methods, or systems to prevent their bonus competitors who rely on these systems from earning a bonus. Bonuses like this completely change how you code and structure systems.

An extra dimension is added to coding non-trivial systems in environments like this: you have to build your feature so it's nearly impervious to subtly introduced defects like this. Reusing currently working code can become a huge and expensive liability.

In my book, the only solution if you're ensnared at a tech corp like this: Optimize for the largest bonus you can get for 2-3 years then quickly *get out*. You must get out before the stress impacts you so badly that, like many of my ex-coworkers, it'll take you years to heal.

After 10-15 years working a company like this, fighting endlessly for bonuses, you will not be recognizable to your former friends. You will be a total wreck. Money can't fix this.
