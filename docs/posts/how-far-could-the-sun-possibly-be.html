How far could the sun possibly be?

(This is the third post in a series, though it can be read independently; here are post #1 and post #2.)

Measuring the distance to the Sun is challenging, for reasons explained in my last post. Long ago, the Greek thinker Aristarchus proposed a geometric method, which involves estimating the Moon‚Äôs sunlit fraction on a certain date. Unfortunately, because the Sun is so far away, his approach isn‚Äôt powerful enough; Aristarchus himself underestimated the distance. Here‚Äôs what we‚Äôll consider:

What We Can‚Äôt Learn From Light

Before we make progress, let‚Äôs quickly dispense with an idea that is tempting but won‚Äôt work.

If we could just measure the time that it takes for light to travel from the Sun to Earth, that would directly tell us the distance. If we could just compare the time when the X-rays arrive at Earth to the time they left the Sun, we could multiply that time by the speed of light and get the distance to the Sun. Super easy!

The only problem is that we don‚Äôt know when they left the Sun. We see the X-rays when they arrive at Earth. We don‚Äôt know when they started their journey. And so, we don‚Äôt have enough information, and the idea fails.

More generally, in order to use light directly to measure a distance, we have to know both the start time and the end time. This is what is used by professionals when they bounce a powerful pulse of radio waves (another invisible form of light) off a distant planet and listen carefully with enormous antennas to the response: the time to go out and back, divided by two and multiplied by the speed of light, provides the distance. But you and I can‚Äôt do that ourselves. And there‚Äôs no natural process where we know both the departure time and the arrival time.
