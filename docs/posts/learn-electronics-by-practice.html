The purpose of the pact was to maintain a balance of power among Lebanon's diverse religious communities, thereby promoting social stability and peaceful coexistence in the newly independent state.

The sectarian distribution of power that was enshrined in the Lebanese constitution, although not explicitly mentioned as the National Pact of 1943, was based on a consensus between the Maronite Christian President Bishara el-Khoury and Sunni Muslim Prime Minister Riad el-Solh. They agreed that Lebanon would be neither a purely Arab state nor a Maronite state, that it would remain neutral in international conflicts, and that the key government positions would be distributed based on the 1932 census which showed a Christian majority.

These decisions were predicated on the assumption that a balance of power among the various religious communities was essential to Lebanon's stability. The founders of Lebanon's modern political system believed that this approach would prevent any single religious group from dominating the country's politics, thus keeping sectarian tensions under control.

However, the decision to codify these arrangements into the constitution was not made without trade-offs, and these became increasingly apparent over time. The National Pact and the confessional system were essentially a form of power-sharing agreement. While this was designed to prevent conflict and maintain stability, it also reinforced sectarian divisions, rather than fostering a broader, more inclusive Lebanese identity.

Furthermore, the confessional system tended to promote political deadlock and inefficiency, as government positions were often filled based on religious affiliation rather than merit. This system of political patronage, in turn, contributed to widespread corruption and economic mismanagement.

There was also the issue of the 1932 census on which the power-sharing agreement was based. With the Christian community in the majority at that time, the arrangement was heavily skewed in their favor. However, the demographic balance in Lebanon shifted over the ensuing decades, leading to increased tensions among communities who felt underrepresented.

In retrospect, it's clear that the decision to codify the National Pact into the Lebanese constitution involved significant trade-offs. While the arrangement did promote stability and prevent any single religious community from dominating in the early years of Lebanon's independence, it also entrenched sectarian divisions, fostered corruption and economic mismanagement, and became a source of tension as Lebanon's demographic balance evolved.
