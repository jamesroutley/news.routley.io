Mitochondria as you've never seen them

The month’s sharpest science shots, selected by Nature’s photo team.

Night-sky spectacular. Photographer Jānis Paļulis was fortunate to capture both the aurora and the Milky Way over this field in Bauska, Latvia. The shot was one of 25 highlighted entries in the Northern Lights Photographer of the Year competition.

Jānis Paļulis

Powerhouses of the cell. This video of a human bone-cancer cell shows its three nuclei (blue —probably the result of failed cell divisions), actin cytoskeleton (red) and a network of moving mitochondria (yellow). It was captured using a confocal microscope by cell biologist Dylan Burnette at Vanderbilt University in Nashville, Tennessee. Burnette says that mitochondria often move around the cell and interact with other structures, and that they exist in a mixture of forms. “The common perception of mitochondria as bean-shaped likely stems from textbook and cartoon depictions,” he says, which are in turn based on microscope images of thin slices through them. “These two-dimensional views do not fully capture the complex and dynamic mitochondrial networks present in most cells.”

Dylan Tyler Burnette

Teeming with life. More than 700 species that are new to science have been documented in the Congo Basin over the past decade, including the electric blue damselfly Umma gumma, the crocodile Mecistops leptorhynchus and the nocturnal frog Congolius robustus. In a report celebrating these discoveries, the conservation group WWF, based in Gland, Switzerland, calls for stronger conservation efforts to protect the region’s ecosystems. “I spent my early career carrying out wildlife surveys in many parts of the Congo Basin and was always astonished by its biodiversity,” said biologist Allard Blom, WWF vice-president for African forests. "We need to do more, as the Congo Basin remains one of the least researched places on the planet and urgently needs additional protection.”

Crowning moment. A crane lifts part of the the 120-tonne reflector dish onto a giant radio antenna at NASA’s Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex near Barstow, California. The facility is part of the Deep Space Network — a collection of ground-based stations that support interplanetary missions. When completed, the antenna will pick up transmissions from spacecraft such as Psyche, Europa Clipper and Voyager 1. It has taken four years to build and is the fifth of six new antennas to join the network as part of an ongoing upgrade.

NASA/JPL-Caltech

Jurassic highway. Palaeontologists have uncovered hundreds of dinosaur tracks at a limestone quarry in Oxfordshire, UK. The footprints were made around 166 million years ago by long-necked herbivores — most likely Cetiosaurus — and the carnivore Megalosaurus. Scientists hope that studying the prints and trackways, some of which stretch for 180 metres, could reveal new insights about how these creatures moved and interacted, including how fast they walked.

University of Birmingham

Sea of stars. This vast, colourful portrait of our neighbouring galaxy, Andromeda, is a mosaic of more than 600 images that took the Hubble Space Telescope a decade to capture. Zooming in on different sections reveals millions of individually resolved stars, giving the galaxy an almost grainy appearance. Stars in the central part of Andromeda are relatively old and yellowish in colour, whereas younger, blue stars are found in the outer rim.

NASA, ESA, B. Williams (University of Washington)

Spa break. A huge Roman bathhouse — complete with hot and cold rooms, well-preserved frescoes and a plunge pool big enough for 30 people — has been uncovered during a major excavation in Pompeii, Italy. The complex sits at the heart of a grand residence thought to have belonged to one of the ancient city’s wealthiest inhabitants. The remains of a woman and a man were also discovered in the house, along with coins, jewellery, pottery and glassware. “Every day here is a surprise … Sometimes in the morning I come to work thinking that it's a normal working day — and then I discover we found something exceptional,” excavation director Anna Onesti told the BBC, which first reported the find.

Christmas dinner. Elephants, giraffes and other animals were given unsold Christmas trees to snack on in what has become an annual post-holiday tradition at Berlin’s zoo. The conifers provide entertainment as well as food, the zoo’s mammal curator Florian Sicks told Associated Press. “The animals can fight with them, they can rub themselves against them, they can throw themselves over them and do various other things with these fir trees,” he said, “which they are very happy about.”

Tobias Schwartz/AFP/Getty
