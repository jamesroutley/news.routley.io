Having no experience can be better than having the wrong experience

The lack of acknowledgement of the existence of a unjust class system among programmers is a large source of bad advice for programmers. E.g., people often tell new programmers to take a menial programming job to get some experience before going after the job they really want.

I just listened to an interview where the founder of the arguably the largest "change careers to programming" org in the world gave that advice ("maybe for your 3rd or 4th job"!!!!) But that advice only makes sense if you don't look at how people hire for "good" jobs.

I've given people the opposite advice (try for that exciting job you want, and if you can't get it then, sure, take any job). Over 50% of people I've suggested to have been able to get that ML/systems/whatever job at a prestigious company that they wanted to get with no exp.

On the flip side, most people I've seen take the standard advice end up like Mike from https://twitter.com/danluu/status/711858996206903296…. In many ways, having no experience is better than having the wrong experience because people don't unfairly prejudge you for having the wrong experience.

When I tried to get my first programming job, I got zero responses from major software companies. I accidentally did the right thing when I took a hardware job I was referred into at Google, because having a prestigious company on my resume was the right programmer class marker.

The people I worked with at a small no-name hardware are still, to this day, the best 100+-person org I've worked with; in terms of actual skill development, my skills came from there, but "small no-name company hardware eng" is the wrong class and "Google eng" is not.

The advice people give makes sense. Taking any programming job will increase your skills and and should therefore make it easier to get the programming job you want. But programmer hiring does not make sense, so advice that makes sense is worthless.
