A few things I’ve come to believe in my years in music tech

A few things I’ve come to believe in my <mumble mumble> years in music tech: 1. Music is not “inherently social” - it is just as often anti-social 2. The number of people that care about what you listen to is very small - and very short lived 3. Discovery is not a viable product

4. There are very smart people at all of the organizations you claim “just don’t get it” - they get it, it’s just they have different inventives 5. Middlemen are not inherently evil - they are desired if they can provide more value than they extract

6. Understanding incentives (both organizational and personal/employee), the flow of money and IP in the current industry is not something you can dismiss as irrelevant

7. Finding new ways to align incentives and/or create new incentives for all of the stakeholders is everything.

8. If you are building tools for DIY artists that can’t grow to support larger teams around the artist as they find success they you have painted yourself into a tiny corner. 9. For industry facing tools, invest in roles and permissions earlier than you think you need to (see #8)

10. There is no single “right way” an artist should run their business. Everyone has different goals. If you want to be a global pop star, you should probably try to get a deal with a major. If you want to super-serve your existing fans, not so much.

11. Learn about the music publishing side of the business 12. Learn about how songwriters and producers work and how they make money

13. Invest in getting good/clean metadata from artists, songwriters, distributors, labels 14. Think of an artist as an organization, not an individual 15. Remember that individuals have aliases 16. Geographic territories matter, a lot

17. The music/tech graveyard is *full* of product ideas just like yours. Spending a little time learning about them and why they failed at the time is a high ROI activity.

18. Make sure you sort out your licensing (recorded, publishing, public performance) early. Licensors will happily wait until your startup becomes big and has raised a lot of money before they come looking for you to make-good. At that point you have no leverage.

100%. What some think are potential products, or potential companies, should really only exist as features.
