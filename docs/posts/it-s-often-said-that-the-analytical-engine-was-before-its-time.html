It's often said that the Analytical Engine was before its time

It's often said that the Analytical Engine was before its time, but that's feels only half-true. Yes, the AE ran into challenges that were nightmares for a mechanical impl, and that first electromechanical and then electronic devices would solve, but that's missing the point.

What stands out to me in retrospect is how _unnecessary_ many of these challenges were. One of the main problems is that the AE was designed to use decimal arithmetic on 40-digit numbers, the equivalent of nearly 133 bits, which of course made the arithmetic unit hard to build!

Even though the AE was capable of programmable control flow and conditional branching and thus perfectly capable of implementing multi-precision arithmetic in software which renders the whole point moot.

Babbage and consorts were not alone in making that mistake; early digital computers likewise started out special-purpose and descended from mechanical calculators and used quite wide arithmetic, often decimal/BCD.

Even the ones that were fully programmable hadn't grasped the implications yet. It took quite a while for accumulators/registers to shrink because well, if you need more, you can always do it in software!

"Real" computers stayed fairly wide. It wasn't until the 70s (Intel 4004, 1971; Intel 8008, 1972; Motorola 6800, 1972; soon many others) that we saw commercial narrow microprocessor designs, chiefly driven by small number of gates possible to fit into an IC at the time.

At that point, the idea of doing any actual wide math in SW wasn't alien anymore, and these designs were what they were chiefly because they had a size and budget target to hit (to go into early electronic calculators, ironically).

What would the "8-bit" version of an AE that leaned more on its programmability look like? Probably just 2 or 3 decimal digits, probably no HW multiply (AE had HW square roots!!), and the actual arithmetic not "in HW", but more like a subroutine library.

I would expect especially so if the "ISA" was designed to make this easy, which of course it would be, in a machine that was always meant chiefly for automatic computation.

So it seems to me that while the AE design was past the time's mechanical engineering, knowing what we do now, you could likely come up with a version that could be realistically built (and kept running reasonably well) with 19th century tech.

What's necessary is the insight (that in the real world took the computing industry years and several iterations to learn) that once you have programmability, that can run the show and your actual arithmetic HW can be tiny and still deliver what you want.
