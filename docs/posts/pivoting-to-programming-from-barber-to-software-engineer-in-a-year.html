Pivoting to Programming: From Barber to Software Engineer in a year

Covid-19 forced me to end my 9-year-long cosmetology career when California's shelter-in-place took effect.

Although I had a part-time marketing role at a small business, it was a massive loss of immediate income. Leaving the cosmetology world was also mentally and physically a tremendous challenge. It was a space that gave me comfort. After spending years, and hours behind the chair - I could give someone a straight razor shave without even thinking - it was second nature.

Like most people stuck at home during the pandemic, I tried to re-frame my fear of change and begin to think about what was next - which meant wrapping up my B.A. in Technical Communication and leaving cosmetology behind. I spent most of 2020 solidifying my knowledge (getting certified in project management, digital marketing, and technical writing) with the hopes that I would land my first corporate job in technical communication (before securing my degree).

After countless rejections from companies all over the Bay Area, I was offered another marketing role for a small Black-owned food start-up in Seattle. However, as much as I enjoyed what I was doing creatively, it didn't fulfill me, and I knew I needed a change professionally.

Months later, I was offered a remote position at a digital media holdings company in Charlotte, NC (after randomly replying to a recruiter on LinkedIn, ha!). At that point in my interviewing journey, I was desperate to accept any opportunity at a larger organization, so I took the offer and started working as a campaign analyst.

It was nothing like I expected. My day-to-day consisted of launching advertising campaigns, dealing with salespeople and gaming clients, and being responsible directly for nearly 10k$ -100k$ of profit every day. I knew ad operations wasn't for me, but fortunately, I was at a company where I had hundreds of other internal opportunities, so I poked around.

Initially, I found a job change program for employees that wanted to switch from their current career to a data-analyst path. I thought about it heavily, mainly because the one critique I received from recruiters during my rejection spree was that I had creative skills but needed more extensive analytical abilities. Yet when I thought about it, I didn't have any passion for analytics or marketing, and I wanted my next career to be something I could immerse myself in.

So I turned to the next program, the Internal Tech Academy, or ITA for short - which directed employees to self-teach themselves to code (using a pre-built curriculum) in the hopes of landing an internal entry-level software engineering job after eight months.

Engineering had always been that "if only in another life" career for me. During undergrad, I found a deep interest in Ethical AI, the impact of technology segregation in Black communities, and the sheer power of programming. While in digital marketing, web development was also a space that piqued my interest. At most, I thought I could become a technical writer or project manager and work closely with the software, but I never imagined the opportunity to become an engineer in less than a year - but I did. I completed the long, arduous, mentally-draining program and became a professional software engineer.

Now, what exactly happened in those eight months? That's a story for another post - this one here.
