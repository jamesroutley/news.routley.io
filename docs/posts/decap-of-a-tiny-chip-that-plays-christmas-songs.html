Decap of a tiny chip that plays Christmas songs

Holiday decap of a tiny chip that plays Christmas songs. The UM66T melody chip looks like a transistor but contains a 64-note ROM and drives a speaker directly. It was designed for greeting cards and toys. Let's see what's inside this chip.🧵

I dissolved the transistor-like package in boiling sulfuric acid to expose the 1.8mm×1.8mm silicon die inside. This was my first acid decap and it turned out okay, although there are some scratches on the die. Under the microscope, you can see the chip's CMOS circuitry.

The 64 notes are stored in a 64×6 ROM. Each note is 4 bits for the frequency and 2 bits for the time. By changing the metal layer, they can program different songs into the chips, selling different versions. At the top of the ROM is a binary decoder. Note the pattern of 8,4,2,1.

Another ROM defines the frequencies for 16 notes. The 32 kHz clock is divided down to generate the note pulses. Instead of a counter, dividing is done by a 7-bit linear-feedback shift register. The ROM values are the right pseudo-random values to initialize the shift register.

Each shift register stage is a two-stage flip flop made from 26 transistors. The inverter loop holds a bit until it is passed to the next stage. Using shift registers instead of normal counters saved a few transistors.

I've labeled the die photo with the functional blocks. The melody chip is a minimal design. It just uses flip flops and gates, not the microcontroller you might expect. The chip has 3 pins, but there are 8 pads here. The others are for fast testing instead of playing the tune.

To generate the 32 kHz clock, the chip has a simple resistor-capacitor-inverter oscillator. The white rectangle is the capacitor. The green zig-zag is the resistor. The resistance can be adjusted by shorting out part of it in the metal layer. White zig zags: transistor bases.

The on-chip R-C oscillator is cheap but inaccurate, unlike a quartz crystal oscillator. If the voltage changes, the frequency changes. In this video, I mess up the songs by lowering and raising the voltage.

It's pretty cool how they fit everything into the melody chip; you just add a battery and a speaker to make it work. (A piezo speaker would be more compact for a card.) When power is applied, the chip goes through its tunes and then stops. I think the chip was introduced in 1990.

The chip is built from CMOS, like most modern ICs. Here's an inverter: a PMOS transistor on the left and an NMOS transistor on the right. The PMOS one turns on with a 0 input, pulling the output high. The NMOS transistor turns on with a 1, pulling the output low. Thus, inverter.

For the high-current output pins, much larger transistors are used. The zig-zag structure of the transistor's gate makes the layout more efficient.
