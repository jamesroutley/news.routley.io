Show HN: Mediator.ai – Using Nash bargaining and LLMs to systematize fairness

Six months in, Daniel ran out of savings. He took a delivery job — about $2,100/month, after fuel — to stay solvent. He's still in the kitchen at 5am every weekday, roughly 25 hours a week. For the past 18 months, Maya has put in 60-hour weeks on everything else: staff, suppliers, books, the Instagram that went 400 → 11,000 followers, the menu work that drove monthly revenue from $7,000 to $22,000.

An investor now wants 20% for $80k (valuing the bakery at $400k). He won't wire the money until Maya and Daniel clarify who owns what. Maya says 70/30: she's put in 2.5x the bakery hours. Daniel says 50/50 stands: he covered $4,800 of her rent out of his delivery income when her savings ran out, and left $23,000 of his distributions in the business over the last 13 months so the cash flow could work. They can't agree. They're about to lose the deal.

Maya and Daniel each walked through their side with Mediator privately. The assistant pressed each of them for the figures that mattered — monthly rent, actual weekly hours, delivery income, distributions declined, revenue growth — before drafting anything. Then Mediator got to work: generating candidate agreements, pitting them against each other, scoring each against both sides' needs, round after round, until no new draft could do better.

What it surfaced was something neither of them had proposed — and something neither would walk away from:

Every number, accounted for.

No handshake. No mutual waiver. Each contribution either side made gets its own line:

  • $4,800 → Maya owes Daniel, formally. The rent he covered out of his delivery income. If she repays within 24 months, Daniel's equity ticks up 2%.
  • $12,000 → Daniel receives, over 24 months. Recognition for the distributions he declined. $6,000 from the investor tranche, the rest from profits quarterly.
  • $1,800/month → Maya's management stipend. Paid before profits, for the operational work Daniel doesn't do. Unwinds as contributions equalize.
  • 1% equity per 200 extra bakery hours → Daniel's earn-back, up to +5%. A continuous meter, not a pass/fail milestone; he picks up ownership hour by hour as the work rebalances. Combined with the rent repayment above, his ceiling is 45%.

The starting equity moves from 50/50 to 62/38 — but the split is what you notice last. The old handshake was incomplete. A 60/40 compromise would be a grudge machine. This is neither: every dollar owed, every hour uncounted, every distribution declined has its own answer.
