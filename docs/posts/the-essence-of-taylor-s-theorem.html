The essence of Taylor's theorem

2024 May 12

\( \def\d#1/d#2{\mathchoice {\frac{d{#1}}{d{#2}}} {d{#1}/d{#2}} {d{#1}/d{#2}} {d{#1}/d{#2}}} \newcommand{\taylor}[1]{\tau_{#1}} % Taylor monomial \)

The aim of this brief note is to distill Taylor’s theorem to its essence and expose its source—the noncommutativity of differentiation and integration. I make no great claim to originality. But I think the fundamental role of noncommutativity has never been made quite explicit in standard treatments of Taylor’s theorem.

1. The operator basis

Recall that Taylor’s theorem (with integral remainder) is the equality

\begin{equation} f(x)-f(0) =Df(0)x+\dots+D^n\!f(0)\frac{x^n}{n!} +\int_0^xD^{n+1}\!f(s)\frac{(x-s)^n}{n!}\,ds \label{taylors-theorem}\tag{T} \end{equation}

for any sufficiently differentiable (real-valued) function \(f\) of a real variable \(x\). The symbol \(D\) denotes the derivative operator \(d/dx\).

The key to understanding Taylor’s theorem is to recognize that it is not a statement about functions but about operators, namely the derivative operator \(D\), and the (definite) integration operator \(S=\int_0^x\). Indeed, by the fundamental theorem of calculus, the left-hand side of equality \eqref{taylors-theorem} is

\begin{equation*} SDf(x)=f(x)-f(0). \end{equation*}

Equality \eqref{taylors-theorem} is therefore a statement about “decomposing” the product \(SD\).

2. A trivial derivation

Let \([D,S]\) denote the commutator \(DS-SD\). Since

\begin{equation*} DS=1, \end{equation*}

by the fundamental theorem of calculus, we have the trivial identity

\begin{equation*} SD=S[D,S]D+S(SD)D. \end{equation*}

Recursive substitution of \(SD\) on the right-hand side yields the identity

\begin{equation} SD=\sum_{k=1}^nS^k[D,S]D^k+S^n(SD)D^n \quad\text{(for all $n\ge1$).} \label{taylors-identity}\tag{T$'$} \end{equation}

This is Taylor’s theorem: To recover equality \eqref{taylors-theorem}, simply apply each side of \eqref{taylors-identity} to \(f\).

3. Noncommutativity at root

The merit of the operator approach to Taylor’s theorem is that it pinpoints the source of the theorem.

Taylor’s theorem is a consequence of the noncommutativity of differentiation and integration, that is, the non-vanishing of the commutator

\begin{equation*} [D,S]=DS-SD=1-SD. \end{equation*}

For if \([D,S]\) were zero, the identity \eqref{taylors-identity} would degenerate to the vacuous identity \(1=1\).

4. Explicit terms

The linearity of \(S\) is crucial to calculating the explicit terms of equality \eqref{taylors-theorem} from the operator identity \eqref{taylors-identity}. Here is a sketch of that calculation.

4.1. The monomials

It is easy to see that

\begin{equation*} S^k[D,S]D^k\!f=D^k\!f(0)\,\taylor k,\quad \text{where $\taylor k(x)=\frac{x^k}{k!}$,} \end{equation*}

since \([D,S]f=f(0)\) and \(S^k1=\taylor k\).

4.2. The remainder

It is not as easy (but still not too hard) to see that

\begin{equation} S^n(SD)D^n\!f(x) =S^{n+1}(D^{n+1}\!f)(x) =\int_0^xD^{n+1}\!f(s)\,\taylor n(x-s)\,ds. \label{remainder-term}\tag{R} \end{equation}

To calculate \(S^{n+1}f\), use the integration-by-parts formula (aka Leibniz rule),

\begin{equation*} S(Sf\,g)=Sf\,Sg-S(f\,Sg). \end{equation*}

It gives

\begin{align*} S^2f &=S(Sf\,\taylor0)\\ &=Sf\,S\taylor0-S(f\,S\taylor0)\\ &=Sf\,\taylor1-S(f\taylor1). \end{align*}

This pattern applies to computing \(S^3f\), \(S^4f\), etc. We find that

\begin{equation*} S^{n+1}f=\sum_{k=0}^n(-1)^kS(f\taylor k)\,\taylor{n-k} \quad\text{(for all $n\ge0$).} \label{integral-powers}\tag{R$'$} \end{equation*}

To prove this identity by induction on \(n\), use the equality \(\taylor k\taylor{n-k}=\binom nk\taylor n\) and the binomial theorem.

Finally, to establish equality \eqref{remainder-term}, apply the right-hand side of \eqref{integral-powers} to \(D^{n+1}f\) in place of \(f\) (and, again, use the binomial theorem).
