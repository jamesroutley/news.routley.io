What a Hacker Stole from Me

July 4, 2025 • I’ve always dreamed of offering a small slice of utopia on the Internet; a place I would love to visit myself. You all know by now, myNoise is my attempt to build something positive, in a world that often feels too chaotic.

A couple of days ago, someone (or some entity) tried to attack this website. They sent hundreds of thousands of requests, attempting to inject code into the site. That didn’t work. myNoise isn’t built on a conventional CMS; I wrote everything from scratch. Maybe that helped. But then they changed strategy. They began downloading every single sound file, again and again. Wasting precious bandwidth, just for the sake of it.

I try to be mindful about my footprint, and the footprint of my noises. Every year, I plant trees to offset the energy used by visitors on the site. I don’t talk much about it anymore, because over the years, it has triggered some criticism. And yes, I agree that the methodology can be questioned. But the idea behind it is simply this: trying to behave responsibly.

And then someone comes along and deliberately floods the server. Just to waste. I guess everyone feels the same when faced with an act of vandalism. That’s the deep unfairness, rooted in the law of entropy: it always takes less energy to break something than it took to create it.

Lately, I find this harder and harder to accept that. Maybe it has something to do with my health incident two years ago. I saw how quickly and easily a disease can take hold of your body, and how long and how much effort it takes to recover. The universal law of entropy again. But illness, most of the time, is not caused by anyone. So we accept it and fight. But when harm is caused by another human being, on purpose, it leaves a different feeling.

Yes, of course, there are worse things in life than someone trying to exhaust a server. This blog post isn’t really about that attack. It was just the trigger to the melancholy that I am filled with now. The reminder of something larger: the imbalance between building and destroying, the rigged combat between the good and the bad. How the world seems to get crazier every day… or maybe it’s always been this way, and I’m just getting older.

Not long ago, we found a young pigeon that had fallen from its nest and had been severely injured. We fed him with a syringe for weeks. We cared for him like family—well, with my family name, I can say we are family. Now he flies freely around the neighborhood, but still comes back to visit us. These are the things that makes me happy. The satisfaction of making small positive changes.

But even here, I can't now refrain from imagining myself there might be someone in the neighborhood who doesn’t like birds. Someone who might one day destroy what we tried hard to save.

It might sound unrelated to server attacks—but to me, it is. It is the same melancholy that visits me sometimes.

That hacker didn’t manage to bring the server down. But still, they did steal something from me. My time and energy. And what little zennitude I had left (my wife would tell you I don’t have much to begin with - haha). So yes, if they’re keeping score, they can mark this as a success. Time I had planned to spend making new sounds, shaping quiet ambiences, working on something positive and useful. Instead, I spent it trying to understand what was happening, how to stop it, and how to protect the project from future attacks. Time lost in defense—something that wouldn’t be needed in my utopian world.

But I’ve made a choice. I’ll keep planting trees. I’ll keep making sounds. I’ll keep helping baby pigeons. I’ll keep offering that little shelter of peace that is myNoise, for anyone who needs it.

Let’s keep building things around us, and together, outnumber those who chose the opposite. Because in the end, building gives meaning to life. Destroying doesn’t.

As our user base continues to grow, so does the demand on our servers. Unfortunately, our current shared hosting environment (provided by one.com) is no longer capable of handling the scale and frequency of requests myNoise receives—especially from our mobile app, which fetches certain data files directly from our servers.

This spike in traffic has triggered automatic anti-bot protections (like Proof of Work mode), which are designed to stop malicious traffic—but unfortunately also block legitimate requests from the app. As a result, you may experience missing sounds, broken generators, or a mobile app that simply hangs. We’re as frustrated by this as you are.

Proof of Work (PoW) is a protection mechanism that requires visiting browsers to solve a small JavaScript puzzle before being granted access. It’s designed to stop bots and malicious traffic—but it also breaks access from anything that isn’t a regular browser. That includes PayPal servers trying to notify myNoise after a donation, our own mobile apps trying to fetch essential data, and even the maintenance scripts triggered from my home computer. PoW sees them all as suspicious and blocks them, even though they’re fully legitimate.

Being on a shared server means we don’t have control over these anti-bot protections. So, we’re now migrating to a private server, which we hope will give us the flexibility we need to solve these issues. The migration is already underway, but as with any major infrastructure change, there might be some hiccups. Some functions already have been made inaccessible during the transition, like donations and custom generators in the Community section.

When I say "we", I really mean "me". And that’s the scary part—I’m not a sysadmin, a web server expert, or a backend engineer. This migration to a new environment is a big step for me, and honestly, it’s a bit intimidating. I’m confident I can get through it, but probably not without a few bumps along the way. So please keep your seatbelt fastened for the time being.

I’ll publish a new blog post once the migration is successfully completed. In the meantime, I won’t have the time to answer emails or work on new generators. The situation has become critical, and I need to fully focus on this migration. myNoise is a one-person project driven by passion, and while technical setbacks are never welcome, they’re a sign the project is growing... so let’s try to see it as good news, too ;-) Thanks for your patience and support.

Feb 4, 2025 • Have you ever noticed the Tape Speed feature on myNoise generators? It’s a neat tool available to my patrons, and it has recently been improved. I believe it deserves a little spotlight here in the blog, to help everyone understand its use and encourage more people to use it.

The term "Tape Speed" might seem a bit unusual for a digital player, but it accurately reflects what this feature does. While it could be called "Transposition" since it primarily shifts the pitch of the sounds you hear, it also mimics the effect of speeding up or slowing down a tape or vinyl recording. That’s where the name comes from.

When you play a tape faster, the sound goes quicker and the pitch gets higher. This makes the sound brighter. It doesn’t just boost the high frequencies like an equalizer; it actually shifts all the sounds up, creating new frequencies that weren’t in the original recording. For nature sounds, this can be a smart way to fill in gaps in the spectrum. For example, if you have tinnitus and want to cover it up with nature sounds, changing the tape speed can help match the sounds of insects in the recording to your tinnitus, giving you some relief.

On the other hand, if you decrease the tape speed, everything slows down, resulting in a darker and warmer sound. As you lose some higher frequencies, this can make the recording less fatiguing for your ears. If you’re looking for sounds to help you sleep or meditate, or if you suffer from hyperacusis, reducing the tape speed might be a great option for you.

Tape Speed offers two different experiences depending on the type of soundscape you’re listening to. If the soundscape is musical, you’ll see notes displayed in the musical scale, allowing you to transpose the soundscape by semitones. This feature is helpful for musicians who want to practise along and match the soundscape to the key of their instrument. However, for natural sounds and atonal soundscapes, this level of detail isn’t necessary, so you’ll only have options to speed up or slow down the playback.

There’s also a fascinating feature that many people overlook: the ability to change the speed of individual tracks within the soundscape. This function, called "Transform"", randomly pitches tracks up or down on an individual basis. Each time you use the Transform option on a natural soundscape, it feels like you’ve been transported to a different moment—perhaps a different time of day, season, or weather condition. It’s truly wonderful to experience - you have to try, though sometimes it's subtle. This feature is also available for musical soundscapes, where it transposes individual tracks by octaves, creating bold and interesting remixes (not subtle at all).

In the User Community, patrons frequently use the Tape Speed control when creating their composite soundscapes. I encourage you to check out the Community section and listen to the work of Arend, who excels at crafting dark and slow ambiances, and Sofia, who creatively mixes my original recordings in exquisite ways using tape speed.

Lastly, the Tape Speed section allows you to tune all sounds on myNoise to the 432Hz pitch. By default, myNoise uses the 440Hz standard. To access this feature, simply press 'y' on your keyboard, and the soundscape will be retuned to 432Hz. There’s a lot of discussion surrounding this alternative pitch, often linked to theories about an ancient frequency. However, the audible difference between 440Hz and 432Hz is minimal, and most people likely won’t notice a significant change when pressing the 'y' key. Yet, for some, this adjustment can have a profound impact, especially when using myNoise as a sound healing tool.

I invite you to explore the Tape Speed control, experiment with different settings, and discover what resonates best with you. Remember, sound is a deeply personal experience, and at myNoise, I am here to help you find the perfect ambiance for your needs. Happy listening!

The Tape Speed control is one of those little extra features, available only to patrons. It’s a fun tool that lets you play with the sounds in unexpected ways—just a small thank-you for those who choose to support the site. I’m not a fan of pushy business tactics, so you won’t hear me insisting. But if you enjoy myNoise and feel like giving back, your support truly makes a difference. It helps keep this place running just as it is—simple, honest, and open to all.

March 10, 2021 • This is a big update! Now, every sound generator on myNoise - the website version of myNoise should I specify - is able to compensate for your headphones or speaker's deficiencies, and even your hearing! E-v-e-r-y   s-o-u-n-d!

When myNoise started, calibration had to be performed off-line, and pre-rendered in the sound generator's audio files. This is why it only worked with the so-called calibrated generators, a subset of generators whose spectrum was compatible with that pre-rendering process. Thanks to the recent advances made in modern browsers, it is now possible to move that feature from server side, to the client side - your browser - and make it work on all myNoise sound generators.

For calibration to work, your browser needs first to measure the frequency response of your headphones and your hearing. The sound reaching your brain is a combination of your headphones frequency response and the frequency response of your ears, in series. This is why myNoise compensates for both, simultaneously. Calibration data is acquired on the calibration page. This page is where the engineering magic happens. By carefully following the instructions, you will provide your browser all it needs to compensate for unbalanced frequencies.

You need to perform the calibration process only once, unless you are switching to a new pair of headphones. Then, on each generator page, you select one of the three options available under the iEQ section : 'balanced', 'full' and 'apply to sliders'. The last option is only available on calibrated generators, and implements to old way of calibrating a sound on myNoise.

If your hearing is perfect, but your headphones aren't, the 'full' option is the best. It then will fully compensate for your headphones deficiencies. If you are using good headphones, you won't hear much of a difference. If your headphones are poorly balanced, the result should be more neutral. Nature sounds will suddenly be revealed to you as they were originally captured.

If you own great headphones, but you are suffering from a hearing loss, the full option will probably sound too bright, though it exactly compensates for your hearing loss. Indeed, your brain is now used to deal with this loss. In other words, that loss is now part of what your brain considers to be the baseline, or the neutral sound if you want. Fully compensating for your hearing loss at this point will result in a change that your brain is not ready not accept. This is why the iEQ offers a more conservative setting, which I labelled 'balanced'.

Last but not least, the old calibration protocol reminiscent from the early days of myNoise, is still available when you are listening to a calibrated generator. In such a case, a third option will be listed under the iEQ section. 'Apply to Sliders' will embed the calibration curve to the sliders. With this last method, you don't force your CPU to perform the calibration on the fly. This is why the iEQ will be disabled, once you have decided to move the calibration process to the sliders.

Jan 28, 2021 • myNoise fans enjoy running different generators at the same time in different windows. The result is often rewarding to the ear. This is to be expected: all tonal generators are programmed in the same key – C#. But sharing the same root key is not sufficient to achieve a perfect blending of sound; generators need to share the same scale, too.

A musical scale is a set of notes build on top of a root note. The scale defines the mood of a soundtrack and ultimately the emotions your audience will feel while listening to a track. A major scale feels happy; a minor scale, sad; a pentatonic scale ... oriental, and so on. Each scale comes in its own flavor.

Like ice cream, not all flavors can be mixed together with great results. This explains why – despite sharing the same root note – not every combination of tonal sounds on myNoise works. It is best to avoid mixing happy major and sad minor scales, which will result in some friction between incompatible notes, causing dissonances. When two generators share the same root note (they all do on myNoise) and scale, then we have a perfect match! Like Route 50 and Night Blue for example.

However, as I don't want to run ads either, I have to be creative to find a viable solution. I hope non-donors will understand why they are now be restricted to one single generator at a time (this was the original idea of the website anyway, until people realised they could open many different sounds in different windows!). March 27, 2014 •Our App has been submitted to Apple today, for official release! And should be available for download in the next days.

It is now time to tell you a little more about the upcoming App. We've been working on it for half a year now. Releasing a dedicated App wouldn't have been possible without the help of a professional programmer, who turned out to be a regular user of this website too! He knows what noise generator are for, he uses them on a regular basis, he understands how myNoise noise generators work - I told him all the secrets - and he knows how to program mobile Apps, definitely. This ends up in a remarquable App!

Basically, the myNoise App is the equivalent of the website, but without an Internet connection required. It has been written from scratch: it is not just the website embedded in an App. This means - for example - that it consumes less power and less CPU than running the website from Mobile Safari on your mobile phone, or that sliders are more responsive. It also allows us to offer some neat features such as an alarm and a timer, variable Animation speeds, multiple calibration sets, user presets, and more. Last but not least, the App will play wirelessly, through AirPlay!

I truly wished that the App would share exactly the same philosophy as the website: be entirely free, with the possibility of supporting the ongoing work through donations. We couldn't do that, but we are not far away though. Two reasons for this: we have a professional developer that truly deserves a compensation for his brilliant work, and second, Apple strict policy does not allow to collect funds in a way that by-passes its store. Apple wants its commission, that's fair enough. The best compromise we achieved is the following one: the App will be free and budled with our three most successful noises (and free means ad-free too). This in itself offers much value already: many other apps on the App Store try to make profit out of these exact same sounds, and have no calibration yet to propose. Our goal is to offer all users a high-quality toolbox that will help them in many circumstances: white noise for noise blocking, open offices, tinnitus therapy; rain noise for noise blocking, anxiety management and meditation; and binaural beats for personal growth, mind change and sound therapy. The rest will be available as individual In-App purchases, or as an 'All You Can Eat Hear" option (see the screenshot).

February 20, 2014 • I have been working on a new feature, for donors only. It is still in beta stage, as I suspect that it might not be fully cross-browser compatible (your bug reports are welcome). February 3, 2014 • Some users have been impressed by the depth and quality behind my latest Singing Bowl generator. The quality of the microphones, their position and the silence in the room played equally important roles. Not to mention the quality of the bowls, which - in my case - were handmade in Patan, Nepal. Many microphones positions have been experimented, to finally retain one where each microphone stays on each side of the bowl, separated by the equivalent to the average human inter-aural distance. Doing so simulates a situation that couldn't exist in reality, like if were hearing the bowl on both its sides witch of our two ears (as if our head was hollow with the bowl located inside!) The illusion is superb, and the sound quality when played through headphones, is amazing.
