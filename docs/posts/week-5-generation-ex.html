Week 5: Generation EX

Last week, I promised I’d talk some about code generation in Phoenix, and specifically, in the context of making model objects for my app. And I’m happy to say that it worked out! Eventually.

I started by trying to pair with another Recurser on Phoenix code generation using phx.gen. But I quickly realized that, when I was poking around in there, there were too many concepts I didn’t understand, like contexts and schemas (at least, how schemas work in Elixir). So I took an unusual step for me: last weekend, I blocked off a few hours, started with the first official Phoenix guide, and then read it and all the other guides after that one.

It turned out to be really useful. So many half-understood things, where I poked around docs online and put something into my app that I only kind of understood, became clear as I read the docs, and many things I didn’t even knew existed were revealed. Overall, this taught me a really important point for the future: if I know a piece of coding technology (framework, language, database, whatever) may be significantly different from other technologies I’ve used, I need to read a decently long tutorial on it before using it.

Actually, this is what I normally do, but in the interest of trying something different in the spirit of Recurse (and internally saying to myself “can this web framework be THAT different from all the other web frameworks I’ve used?”, heh), I tried diving in after a little bit of reading. Note to self: don’t do that again!

(As an aside, it’s important to say that, for sustainability (both for me and for my partner), it’s important to me not to take too many weekend / after-hours excursions like reading the docs was. It was very useful this one time, but I know making it a habit will turn out badly for me in the program, personally.)

Armed with the knowledge from the guides, I paired again today with a Recurser, and it went very well! They were very helpful in pointing out lots of bugs and logic errors I was making, and they were especially awesome in discovering embedded schemas for me, because that was just what I needed right now: models without backing database tables.
