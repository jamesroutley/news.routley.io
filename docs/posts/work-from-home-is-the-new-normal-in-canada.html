Work-from-home is the new normal in Canada

  1. NP Comment

Despite what some government and corporate leaders may want, work-from-home is here to stay

Published Mar 31, 2023  •  Last updated 2 days ago  •  5 minute read

Be careful what you wish for: you might actually get it. In the spring of 2020, in the early days of the pandemic, governments around the world, including Canada, pushed harsh measures that have rarely been used in peacetime, including lockdowns and the closing of offices and businesses, except the most essential services. As a consequence, people used to working in offices were told they had to work from home. It was widely seen as an essential but temporary measure to keep people safe during the pandemic. With the benefit of hindsight, we now know that the science behind lockdowns was tenuous at best. Sweden, widely pilloried at the time for refusing to lock down and for allowing people to interact with each other based on common sense, has ended up with an excess mortality rate that is lower than most countries that locked down hard, including Canada.

The Canadian government was among the more enthusiastic when pushing work-from-home. For example, then deputy minister of Transport Canada, Michael Keenan, declared that this would be the “default” mode of working for the “foreseeable future.” Likewise, every government department and major corporations pivoted to a work-from-home model. Far from pushing back, there was generally enthusiastic compliance from the corporate world in the U.S., Canada and elsewhere. For instance, the New York investment bank, Goldman Sachs, set up remote exercise regimes and even cooking classes for employees working from home. Likewise in Canada, major corporations and banks embraced the work-from-home model.

What few, if any, realized in the spring and summer of 2020 was just how popular this model would turn out to be. Workers were now free from long and expensive commutes to work, saving on gas, parking, food costs, and even childcare expenses. If you didn’t like where your company was located, maybe because the rent was too high or you preferred the countryside to the city, you could move somewhere else. Little wonder then, at the reluctance of many workers to return to the office as the pandemic dissipated in 2022 and 2023. The federal government itself hasn’t exactly been able to crack the whip. By March 31, it expect workers to be in the office for only two to three days a week, and even then, exceptions are allowed on a case-by-case basis.

Employers contend there is lower productivity when their employees work from home. The CEO of the Royal Bank of Canada, Dave McKay, has asserted that two plus years of work-from-home has had a negative impact on productivity and innovation. However, the effects of working from home aren’t all negative. Apart from all the obvious cost savings, new research has quantified the time savings associated with working from home. A January 2023 study, published by the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) in the U.S., looked at data from 27 countries and concluded that the average daily amount of time saved in working from home was 55 minutes in the U.S. and 65 minutes in Canada. Of that time saved, the study found that on average people allocated about 40 per cent of the time to their work, 11 per cent to caregiving and the rest to other activities. In other words, even if it’s true that working from home is less productive, people put more time into their work, so it’s hard to know how these effects balance out.

Despite pressure from employers to return to the office, the preference for working from home remains remarkably resilient. A March 2023 study, also published by the NBER, looked at job postings from 2019 to 2023 in the five main English-speaking countries in the developed world. They found that over this period, postings that allowed for remote or hybrid work rose by more than three times in the U.S. and by five times or more in Canada, Australia, New Zealand and the U.K. The research shows that job postings for work-from-home and or hybrid work remain on an upward trajectory in all five countries. In other words, despite what government and corporate leaders may want, work-from-home is here to stay.

Preference for working from home remains remarkably resilient

For those who can do it, remote work has been a blessing. But the reality is that, willy-nilly, the pandemic greatly accelerated the trend toward creating a new class in society — those who can work remotely indefinitely — breaking the link between where you live and where you work that’s been the norm through history. By contrast, harsh lockdowns most adversely affected people in industries where workers had to be proximate to their workplaces, like truck drivers, caregivers, cooks and delivery people, and most health-care providers. These people are potentially a new underclass, without the same lifestyle choices as those whom they are servicing who work remotely. Likewise, there are looming dilemmas when it comes to land use and zoning, given the fact that large office complexes in major cities in all over the developed world are now partially or even largely empty. High vacancy rates of office spaces don’t tell the whole story, because many offices that are rented are still largely empty.

One of the maxims of social science is the law of unintended consequences. In the spring of 2020, when governments were imposing lockdowns, no one expected that the shift to work-from-home would become essentially permanent. Most people expected that lockdowns and work-from-home would last for two weeks, the time it was thought it would take to “flatten the curve.” There’s no prospect that work from home or, at any rate, hybrid work will cease to remain the norm going forward, especially as labour markets remain tight. Perhaps, rather than fighting it, government and corporate leaders ought to accept it as the new normal.
