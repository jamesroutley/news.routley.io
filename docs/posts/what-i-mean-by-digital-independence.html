What I mean by digital independence

Published on May 28th, 2024.

I recently updated the header on my website to reflect my interest in writing about “digital independence.” I want to elaborate a bit about what I mean by this phrase and where I’m trying to go with it.

The kind of digital independence I’m talking about is the idea of liberating our digital lives from the visibility and control of powerful and malicious entities. Specifically: large tech companies, prying government agencies, scrapers for large language models, advertisers, data brokers; you get the idea. I want to find ways to enjoy the marvels of modern computation without contributing to their relentless pursuit of wealth and power at everyone else’s expense.

The goal of this is to discover tangible actions that can be taken by people and communities, at all levels of technical ability, to pursue their own versions of digital independence. Each person and community will have their own path towards this ideal, and this diversity will be the key to collective success, whatever “success” may look like. All the while, I want to prove that these acts of resistance can be rewarding, fun, and tangentially working towards strengthening communities. I want to show that it’s perfectly possible to use technology to suit people’s needs and desires rather than the needs and desires of the powerful. This is a hopeful and constructive narrative that is in stark contrast to the pessimism I find prolific these days, perhaps because it isn’t easily squeezed into a 260-character tweet.

It’s important to acknowledge that we are up against a wicked problem here. There is no explicit end game or point of success where we can “achieve” digital independence. In order to make any kind of progress, we need to make the process of resisting control and carving out our independence be the fun part. There will be no moment of success where we can call it a day and head home. If we feel like we’re constantly walking up an insurmountable mountain, that means we’re doing things right and should keep chugging along.

To be clear, many of my ideas aren’t novel here, but I need to distinguish them from the open source software movement. My interpretation of digital independence does not exclude the use of proprietary or commercial software in the same way the FOSS movement does. I’m willing to take a less purist stance if it furthers the cause of resisting technologies that are weaponized against people and communities. If a company building proprietary software behaves and/or is organized in ways that are friendly to those that buy their products, I welcome their use1.

My contribution to this field thus far has been documenting how to self-host services, getting music suggestions from humans, and my favorite: becoming “illegible.” It’s a small and slow start, but I hope identifying the direction I’m heading in will help me expand further on these ideas. Along these lines, I’ve assembled a bibliography of books, articles, and talks that have been a source of inspiration. They’re worth checking out if any of this resonates with you.

If you’re picking up what I’m putting down here, just know that the summit of the mountain we’re climbing is high and far away, and that’s the good news.
