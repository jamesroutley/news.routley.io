Van life (cancer edition) finale

This is it, folks. “I regret to announce — this is The End. I am going now. I bid you all a very fond farewell.” Things were going so well. I was all but declaring myself immortal when things changed. A routine scan showed cancer was well and truly kicking my arse, and once again, I was forced to plan for a death at the end of April. My flight to [undisclosed sensible European country] has been booked for 19 April, and all that remains is to say my goodbyes.

Imgur, it’s been a wild ride. I want to thank you all for making the last leg so special, and full of humour, love, and drug recommendations. Strangers have turned to friends, and this usually-a-lurker has turned into something of a regular poster.

For my final post, I thought I’d just share a collection of things from my adventure in Dobby. There’s no real theme, and I don’t think there needs to be.

I’ve started to cough up blood (never good) and my mind is failing me. I want to die on my own terms, which is why I’ve chosen to go to [undisclosed sensible European country] whilst I still have the capacity to make my own choice. With my speech and memory going, it’s already difficult to persuade doctors that I’m sufficiently “with it” to understand the gravity of what I’m doing. You can all rest assured that I do understand, and it’s entirely my own choice. I’ve been necessarily reluctant to tell loved ones too much about this final journey, for fear they’ll be prosecuted back in England. So, I am dying on my own.

I’m not afraid to die, but I don’t mind admitting that I don’t feel ready. Picking out my final outfit, final meal, final words, it strikes me as odd that I will suddenly only exist in memory, exactly as I was in that moment.

I think I’ve come to realise that life isn’t about having a good hand. It’s about playing your shitty cards well. I’ve crammed a lot of life into this time, and I’m not looking back with many regrets (although I’d be lying if I said I had none!)

Fighting a terminal illness isn’t on anyone’s five year plan, but once you let go of the expectations you set for yourself, even a shorter life can be a happy and fulfilling one. I’ve felt more alive in the last 6 months than I had in the previous 6 years. It’s all about perspective. Light will trump darkness every time, given the right tools. In my case, my tools were an incurable sense of humour, and, well, you guys.

In the words of Winnie the Pooh, how lucky I am to have something that makes saying goodbye so hard. That silly old bear sure did know a thing or two about living your best life, but my guy Eeyore came up with the best quote of all: “it never hurts to keep looking for sunshine.” I’ve tried so hard to do that, no matter what shite has been thrown at me. Boredom makes you count every second, whilst terminal illness makes every second count. Fill your life with as much passion and wonder as you can muster. Also remember that cancer is not the only terminal illness: fear, poverty, addiction, loneliness. They are all just a lethal as my cancer. Look out for each other, when you can.

A wise man once said, disease intensifies narcissism, especially if it is terminal. Interpreting that as advice rather than critique, I’d like to be self-indulgent for a moment, and share what I hoped my life would be. I trained as lawyer, and had wanted to go into human rights. Things don’t always turn out the way you expect, and I ended up in corporate law, living a lifestyle unworthy of myself, and making the world worse year on year. On a dark day, I sometimes wonder if this is the universe’s way of balancing the wrong I did. I tried my best to be a good and kind person, but ultimately, I know my job was one that made rich motherfuckers even richer. I’m sorry.

I had hoped to get married, maybe adopt a kid or two. I saw myself living on a small farm, with plenty of space to wander and feel free. Away from city air and shitty bosses. Finally able to be myself.

Obviously, that life plan didn’t work out. And that’s okay.

I’ve felt a pressure to “do more” since my diagnosis. Everyone seems afraid of doing too little - whether to treat illness, or crossing off some bucket list cobbled together on a teary Monday night. Most have no appreciation that equally terrible mistakes are entirely possible in the other direction. At this point, doing too much seems to be my greatest danger to happiness. I need to slow myself down and appreciate all that I’ve experienced.

I’ve found the English language is lacking sufficient vocabulary to express the sadness that you’ll make no more memories. I’m not a religious person, so I can’t even process this longing in terms of an afterlife, yet, somehow, I feel I’ll miss the things and people I love. Can you still miss something if you’re not actively experiencing the loss?

I’ve run out of intelligent things to say, so I guess it’s just a well this is my final post. I’ve asked a trusted friend to do a small update when my ashes are blowing in the wind, so you’ll all stay with me, until the very end.

And there it is. Brief, bloody, brutal; my bout with cancer. When the sun goes down, the stars come out. Thanks for seeing me through it all, my friends. Twenty six years has flown by, but what a ride it’s been.
