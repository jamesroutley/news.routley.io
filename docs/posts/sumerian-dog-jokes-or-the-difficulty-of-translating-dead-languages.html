Sumerian dog jokes, or the difficulty of translating dead languages

Let's talk about Sumerian dog jokes as an excuse to embark on a long tedious thread about the peculiarities and difficulties of translating anything from the deadest of dead languages.

Let's take this line by line. ur-gir15-re eš2-dam-še in-kur9-ma /urgir-e ešdam-še i.n-kur-ma/ "A local dog walked into a tavern." The -e on "ur gir" reflects ergative case, indicating the dog is the agent of the verb kur9, "to enter". The -še on "ešdam" is terminative.

As for the verb... Sumerian forms finite verbs by agglutinating prefixes and suffixes to the basic root or stem in a specific order. Here's a handy diagram which covers all the basics:

The main problem with Sumerian verbs is the nature of the writing system. Sumerian cuneiform is morphophonemic. A sign can carry a value equivalent either to a sound, or to a discrete morpheme.

Sumerian scribes were often quite lazy about writing out the whole word. The sequence /munnandu/ - "he built it for him" - common to dedicatory inscriptions, can be written as mu-du3, mu-na-du3, mu-un-du3. Figuring out what they were going for is often a nightmare.

With all this in mind we're finally ready to tackle in-kur9-ma. This is most likely a contraction of i3-ni-kur9-ma. In this case, -ni- functions as a locative with no pronomial prefix, or as a pronomial n + directive i.

In fact, here's a publication which notes that one of two variant texts of this joke/proverb actually preserves the i-ni variant. (Duplicate texts are fairly common, and a primary method of reconstructing the meaning of damaged parts.)

The final -ma represents either a nominalising suffix "-a" plus another morpheme containing an /m/ sound, or it represents a borrowed Akkadian conjunction suffix -ma, essentially meaning "and". Either way it doesn't make a huge difference to the basic translation.

nig2-na-me igi nu-mu-un-du8 nig2-na-me reflects niŋ ("thing") + nam ("any") "anything", this time with a directive or locative-terminative case marker "-e".

gi nu-mu-un-du8 is a compound verb. Compound verbs are formed by pairing a verbal stem with a nominal element, in this case, du8 "spread, caulk" with igi "eye" = "spread the eye" = "see".

nu-mu-un-du8 gives us an example of a modal prefix in action - "nu-" creates the negative mood. mu-un-du8 alone straightforwardly reflects a 3rd person singular animate agent. Put together igi nu-mu-un-du8 = "he sees not". With the noun niŋ₂-na-me, "He doesn't see anything."

Okay now we have to do the difficult part. ne-en ŋal2 taka4-en-e-še ŋal2 taka4 is another compound verb - "taka" probably means something like "throw" in its most literal sense. It is variously used to mean "abandon", "divorce", but also "entrust" or "set aside."

ŋal2 is here used as a nominal element, but is itself a verb, meaning "be (somewhere/available)." We happen to have this compound verb translated into Akkadian as "petûm", "to open". As with many Sumerian compound verbs, you can sort of see how they got there if you squint.

What you might have noticed is that "ŋal2 taka-en-e-še" doesn't look like any of the other verbs we've seen so far. For one thing, it doesn't have a conjugation prefix - in fact, it doesn't seem to have any prefixes.

Interpretations of this line meaning "I will open this one" seem to rest on two assumptions: 1. The conjugation prefix i3 has been omitted (across multiple variants) 2. This verb represents a marû conjugation of the verb "ŋal taka" But now we need another small diversion...

Sumerian doesn't really have "tense" as such. Instead, it has two "tense/aspects" (because Sumerologists don't like to overcommit): "hamtu" and "marû". Hamtu is essentially "perfective", while marû is "imperfective." Complete vs incomplete actions.

The other verbs we've seen so far are hamtu. Marû verbs work slightly differently - whereas in hamtu the agent of the verb occurs in the final prefix before the stem, in marû, the object/patient of a transitive verb sits just before the stem. The agent/subject follows the stem.

They're also often distinguished by the addition of an -e(d)- suffix, which indicates a "future" sense. We can sort of see why someone would want ŋal2 taka-en-e-še to be marû: there's es all over the place, and what looks like a 1st singular -en suffix for the subject.

There are two arguments against this. 1. Different verbs show marû in different ways. Some have suppletive stems, others reduplicate completely or partially, some use a suffix, others don't distinguish between m. and h. ŋal2 taka/taka is established to be a reduplicating verb.

2. We would expect a marû transitive verb to signal its direct object before the verb stem, and we see no sign of that here. So what's going on?

There's one verb form we haven't talked about that doesn't use prefixes at all: the imperative. The imperative is formed by 1. Taking a verb in hamtu aspect. 2. Suffixing all its prefixes to the verb stem - shifting all suffixes to the right in the process.

I think what we're seeing with "ŋal2 taka-en-e-še" is a very confusing imperative. "ŋal i3-taka-aš" - standing for ŋal intaka-eš - would be a perfectly grammatical hamtu verb - "he opened" them. As an imperative ŋal taka-i3-eš would mean "open it!"

So what's with out odd sequence? I think what we're seeing is an odd case of vowel harmony in which the /i/ of the conjugation prefix has assimilated to the /e/ of the following syllables. en-e-še stands for /i3-n-eš-e/. "He [imperative] open them."

The final “e” here represents a particular kind of nominalising particle. Usually this appears following the -ed- “futurising” particle to create something like a latin gerund phrase indicating purpose or intent.

So what should this verb mean? Very roughly, “He should open them.” (ne-en - “this” at the beginning isn’t marked for plural because Sumerian doesn’t regularly mark nouns as plural directly - the marking of the verb does the heavy lifting.)

And now we can finally explain the joke: A friendly dog walks into a bar. His eyes do not see anything. He should open them.

It’s a “man walks into a bar and hurts his head” tier dad joke, basically. The “pun” in Sumerian is centred on the fact that the verb “to see” also literally means “open (one’s) eye”.
