Warriors, starting with the ancient Greeks and the creation of jobs like the Archon, displaced priests as the rulers of society. But then warriors themselves got displaced. And they viewed merchants before that, but kind of like rats.

Even in ancient Rome — Caesar’s era, all these great people competing. How often do you think of Rome? I think about it maybe a normalish amount for a guy, like twice a week. That’s probably an underestimate.

As the Empire was rising and the Republic was falling, an era much like this one in a lot of ways, you had truly exceptional people, and there were a lot more than — Caesar was not the only one. Gaius Marius alone was his opponent, but there was also the other two of the triumvirate, Mark Antony, and Crassus. Crassus is the one I’m thinking of. He was the merchant and they viewed him almost like a rat riding a unicycle. They weren’t even mad that he was so rich, they were just very confused — how’s this even possible?

He could be almost as wealthy as the whole Roman treasury, 200 million sesterces. But he got no honor. Crassus died in the pursuit of honor because he tried to pursue a military campaign in Parthia, the Persian Empire. He might have had some skills, but he was no Caesar. Caesar himself was actually planning to go to Parthia as well, using avenging Crassus as the excuse to justify it.

Then as merchants themselves have gotten replaced — sort of replaced by, let’s say, engineers broadly speaking. But then engineers themselves are being replaced by the meta-programming engineers, just software engineers. It’s the software eating the world thing taken across a big arc of history.

This era might be a return to the bronze age. In the bronze age was maybe the first time in history that any individual could acquire way more than they could consume.
