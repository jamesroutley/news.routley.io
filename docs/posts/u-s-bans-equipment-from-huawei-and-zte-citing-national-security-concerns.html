U.S. Bans Equipment from Huawei and ZTE, Citing National Security Concerns

Text size

The U.S. government on Friday banned electronic equipment from several companies based in China, citing national security concerns.

Among the telecommunications and surveillance products banned are those produced by Huawei Technologies and ZTE Corp.  (ticker: 000063.China)

The Federal Communications Commission on Friday said it had adopted rules banning communications equipment “deemed to pose an unacceptable risk to national security from being authorized for importation or sale in the U.S.,” according to an FCC press release. The order applies to communications equipment made by Huawei and ZTE . Equipment made by Hytera Communications (002583), Hangzhou Hikvision Digital Technology (002415.China), and Dahua Technology (002236.China), used for purposes like public safety or security of government facilities was also included.

In a statement, FCC chairwoman Jessica Rosenworcel called the rules “comprehensive.”

“The action we take today covers base station equipment that goes into our networks,” Rosenworcel said. “It covers phones, cameras, and Wi-Fi routers that go into our homes. And it covers re-branded or ‘white label’ equipment that is developed for the marketplace.”

The rules were adopted on Friday, but the change was several years in the making. In 2020, the FCC called both Huawei and ZTE  national security threats. The FCC’s announcement today puts into action a law signed last November by president Joe Biden, according to the release.

Huawei declined to comment. ZTE, Hytera, and Dahua couldn’t be reached for comment. Hangzhou Hikvision didn’t immediately comment.
