Preface

In today‚Äôs continuation of my saga against ridiculous app sizes, I take a look at United Airlines' iOS app.

There I was, boarding my United plane and walking past the rows and rows of seats, when I finally noticed none of them had built-in headrest screens. I swear I double-checked this before booking my ticket. As I sat down, an intercom announcement came on reminding passengers to download the United app if they wanted access to in-flight movies and entertainment. This wasn‚Äôt the first time I had seen this, Hawaiian Airlines pulled the same stunt on me before. It‚Äôs becoming somewhat of a popular trend in the airline industry to cut costs by having the passenger bring the hardware, and as an added bonus they get to force their way into your coveted list of installed apps. No worries then, I‚Äôll download their app and be on my way‚Ä¶

‚Ä¶ wait, it‚Äôs 439MB?! This can‚Äôt be right.

Note: as mentioned in my previous article, download and install sizes are a tricky subject for iOS. Apple heavily compresses the download size (which is great!) but doesn‚Äôt make this easy to find. Apple also supports incremental downloads, meaning you can download a ‚Äúdiff‚Äù of app binaries in some cases (also amazing!), but this is even harder to measure on an actual device. In my case I was trying to download the United app for the first time on a runway before my flight took off.

Interesting! It looks like none of the Swift framework symbols have been stripped, severely bloating our binary size. We can actually see United‚Äôs CI pipeline as part of the symbol name. ü§£

Like I said before, none of these symbols are actually needed. Let‚Äôs whip up a Bash script to run the frameworks through strip:

#!/bin/bash

function strip_framework() {
    FRAMEWORK_NAME=$(basename "$1")
    strip -rSTx -no_code_signature_warning "$1" -o "StrippedFrameworks/$FRAMEWORK_NAME"
}

function copy_framework() {
    FRAMEWORK_NAME=$(basename "$1")
    cp -- "$1" "OriginalFrameworks/$FRAMEWORK_NAME"
}

export -f strip_framework
export -f copy_framework

# I couldn't figure out how to 'find' for only binary files =[
find "$1" -perm +111 -type f -not -name "*.js" -not -name "*.png" \
-not -name "*.json" -not -name "*.svg" -not -name "*.plist" -not -name "*.md" -not -name "*.sh" \
-exec bash -c "strip_framework \"{}\"; copy_framework \"{}\"" \;

Learnings

While the United dev team should have noticed this much earlier ‚Äì I originally started writing this in August 2021 but life happened ‚Äì it is fair to say this area of app development is complicated to understand.

My biggest takeaway is that the developer tooling remains extremely poor for monitoring app sizes. The App Store should be sending all sorts of red flags to developers that have such easy fixes to make. It begs the question of whether Apple is even incentivized to make the tooling better. I‚Äôll give a shoutout to Emerge Tools as the only startup I‚Äôm aware of trying to tackle this problem.

Anyway, I hope you had fun reading this! And I really hope this gets fixed and saves people real bandwidth and stress on the runway!
