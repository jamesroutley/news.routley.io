A Wordle clone in 50 lines of Bash

It seems like the word list in /usr/share/dict/words contains some non-English words

Commonly, /usr/share/dict/words is a symlink to the locale of your machine. Here's my simplified result, for example (Ubuntu)

.rw-r--r--  972k root 25 apr  2018  american-english
.rw-r--r--  968k root 25 apr  2018  british-english
.rw-r--r--  492k root 31 mrt  2020  cracklib-small
.rw-r--r--  4,2M root  7 jan  2018  dutch
lrwxrwxrwx     5 root  7 jan  2018  nederlands -> dutch
.rw-r--r--   199 root 15 nov  2018  README.select-wordlist
lrwxrwxrwx    30 root 20 jun  2019  words -> /etc/dictionaries-common/words
lrwxrwxrwx    16 root 25 apr  2018  words.pre-dictionaries-common -> american-english

So, if you have this symlinked to e.g. 'dutch', you'd, naturally get mostly non-english words.

Yet in e.g. american-english are at least some words with non-ascii characters (counting all, including <> 5 char words:)

grep -c -P -n "[\x80-\xFF]" /usr/share/dict/american-english
256

The remainders are arguably non-English. As one can argue that the fact they are in the dictionary, makes them English. Not that the /usr/share/dict/ is any sort of official dictionary, it still is one nonetheless. I'd argue it is more reputable than a random words.txt from github, though.

Edit: maybe interesting tidbit:

diff --minimal --side-by-side --suppress-common-lines  /usr/share/dict/american-english /usr/share/dict/british-english  | wc -l
3259

There are at least 3259 words (of any length) that are both English but only appear in one of both british or american dictionary. This makes "non-english" even more difficult to define :D
