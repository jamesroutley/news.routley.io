Â BEFORE FM, THERE WAS APEX

Â In the early and mid-1930s, radio communication was confined to the Low Waves (100-500 kHz), Medium Waves (500-1500 kHz), and the Short Waves (1,500 to 30,000 kHz).Â  The frequencies above that, referred to as the “ultra-high frequencies”, were truly the “Wild West” of radio.Â  It was a place for experimentation and a possible home to future radio services.Â  Commercial broadcasting to the public took place entirely on the standard broadcast band (540-1600 kHz), but it was affected by a number of defects that annoyed the public – natural and man-made static, local and skip interference, atmospheric fading, and limited fidelity.Â  Starting about 1932, a number of brave and daring broadcasters sought permission from the FCC to conduct experiments in the Ultra-Short Waves in an attempt to find solutions to these problems.Â  In particular, these experimental stations wanted to transmit wideband, high fidelity audio.Â  Amplitude modulation, the only known method of transmitting audio at the time, was the method utilized on these so-called “Apex” stations.Â  Experimental licenses were being issued for up to 1,000 watts on frequencies at 25-26 MHz and 42 MHz.Â  By 1939, these Apex stations were operating in 34 U.S. cities in 22 statesÂ  Â They suffered less skip interference than standard AM stations, but static was still a problem.

Â At the same time that these Apex broadcasters were gaining a foothold in the upper frontiers of the radio spectrum, an entirely new type of radio service was also being demonstrated - one that was destined to cause Apex AM to become obsolete.Â  That service was called Frequency Modulation, or FM.

Â MAJOR ARMSTRONG’S AMAZING INVENTION

Â Contrary to popular belief, Major Edwin Howard Armstrong did not invent FM. Â The first patent for frequency modulation had been filed as far back as 1902.Â  In the 1920’s, RCA had experimented with FM, and found it lacking.Â  However, all of this work had been done using narrowband deviation. Â In 1922,Â  Bell Labs engineer John R. Carson published a mathematical study that indicated FM was less frequency efficient than AM, and subject to heavy distortion.Â  In his 1922 paper to the Institute of Radio Engineers (I.R.E.), Carson wrote:Â  “This type of modulation inherently distorts, without any compensating advantages whatsoever.”Â  This and other “expert” papers effectively closed the book on further investigation of FM for a number of years.Â 

Meanwhile, in his research at Columbia University, Armstrong had long been searching for a cure for the problem of radio static, but he had come up empty-handed.Â  Once he finally concluded that it was impossible to separate intelligence from static with conventional amplitude modulation, he took another look at frequency modulation.Â  His truly revolutionary breakthrough was his discovery of “Wideband FM”.Â  Up until that time, all radio research had the goal of narrowing the bandwidth – trying to squeeze more intelligence into a smaller space.Â  Armstrong took the opposite approach - he discovered that the very wide deviation of the frequency delivered vastly superior audio performance -- high fidelity audio and a high signal to noise ratio.Â  Also, by spreading the intelligence over a larger frequency range, less transmitter power was required to achieve the desired range.Â  Furthermore, FM signals were much more immune to interference than AM due to its “capture effect” – an interfering signal needed to be more than 50% the strength of the desired signal to cause audible interference, compared to 5% or less with AM.Â  This characteristic would considerably reduce the required separation between stations occupying the same channel and allow more channel re-use, which compensated for its greater occupied bandwidth.Â  And most importantly, because all natural and man-made static is amplitude modulated, FM proved to be amazingly noise-free.Â  Armstrong improved its resistance to noise still further by incorporating a new receiver component – a limiter that stripped off the amplitude variations in the received signal before it reached the detector.Â  He had finally solved the problem of static interference that had confounded radio experts since the beginnings of the art.

It would been impossible to use Armstrong’s wideband FM system in the commercially active radio spectrum at that time (200 kHz to 30 MHz) – one 200 kHz wide FM channel alone occupied the space of twenty channels in the standard AM broadcast band.Â  But the “ultra-high” spectrum above 40 MHz was virgin frontier, and it had plenty of room to accommodate spectrum-hungry services like FM radio and television.

Â Major Armstrong first demonstrated his revolutionary technology privately to David Sarnoff and the engineers of RCA in 1933 and 1934.Â  Then, when RCA failed to show an interest in licensing his technology, he gave his first public demonstration to the I.R.E. On November 5, 1935, its members heard amateur station W2AG operating in FM at a distance of 17 miles from the meeting location.Â  The following May, the IRE publishes Armstrong’s technical paper "A Method of Reducing Disturbances in Radio Signaling by a System of Frequency Modulation".

Â All who heard FM for the first time were astonished at its clarity and robustness.Â  Nonetheless, while clearly superior to AM in all regards, the industry’s response to FM was mixed.Â  Although light years beyond the capabilities of standard AM broadcasting, it was not adaptable to the current structure of broadcast radio.Â  FM’s implementation would require a complete new infrastructure of broadcasting stations and millions of new consumer receivers.Â  FM could also potentially make obsolete more than 600 AM broadcasting facilities in the United States, destroying huge investments the major players had made in the broadcasting industry.Â  After surviving its financially challenging beginnings, radio broadcasting of the thirties was proving to be extremely lucrative, and the dominant big broadcasting corporations were not interested in seeing FM upend their business plans.Â  As a result, even as they gave positive lip service to FM, the powerful radio interests, led principally by David Sarnoff and RCA, quietly aligned their strategies to suppress the deployment of Armstrong’s FM technology.Â 

DEMONSTRATION TO THE FCC

In 1934, RCA had permitted Armstrong to conduct FM tests from its facility atop the Empire State Building, but in 1936 he was asked to vacate to make room for television tests.Â  Undeterred, he requested permission from the FCC to construct his own high power FM station, and, although initially rebuffed by Commission staffers, he was finally granted permission to build a 40 kW experimental station in June of 1936.Â  The construction of this station was a multi-year project, which included building a giant 400 ft. tower on the Palisades at Alpine, New Jersey, near the George Washington bridge.

Â Finally, on January 11, 1939, Armstrong’s station was ready to demonstrate FM’s capabilities to the world.Â  He invited FCC engineers to monitor the station at Sayville, New Jersey, 50 miles away from the transmitter site, operating on 42.8 MHz with 20 kW. Â FM NETWORKS

Â One of the most promising facets of FM broadcasting was its ability to create wireless regional networks by relaying the programs captured off air from other FM stations.Â  Because of FM’s superb audio quality, a program could be passed between stations in multiple hops and still have a quality that was vastly superior to the big AM networks.Â  In January, 1940, a program was relayed in six hops from Yonkers to Alpine, to Meriden and on to Worchester, through Paxton and Mount Washington and finally it was broadcast by a Boston AM station.Â  This prospect was another confidence-shaker for the big time broadcasters, who were paying huge sums to AT&T for their network lines.Â  These high distribution cost, plus the limited availability of these lines, gave the national networks a semi-monopoly by restricting the entry of others into the network business.Â  But now, groups of modestly-financed FM stations could form an alliance to create regional and even national networks having much better audio quality at a substantially lower cost!Â  This possibility represented a colossal threat to the big AM networks as well as to AT&T.

Â POST-WAR FREQUENCY MOVE CRIPPLES FM

Â In 1945, the FCC began a series of hearings into the post-war use of the high frequency spectrum.Â  On January 15, 1945, the FCC announced its proposal to move FM band to 88 to 108 MHz so it could reassign the pre-war FM band to television.Â Â  A bitter battle ensued, with the FM interests fighting the television interests, and with the FCC apparently entrenched in the TV camp.Â  Then a new issue was raised by the FCC. Â POST SCRIPT

Â In the end, the superior capabilities of Major Armstrong’s system of frequency modulation triumphed over the entrenched interests that had tried to suppress it.Â  Even though they successfully set back FM’s growth for twenty years, in the end its tremendous technical and sonic advantages could not be denied.

Â One wonders what would have happened if FM had been allowed to continued its trajectory on the original 42-50 MHz band.Â  On one hand, the pioneer stations enjoyed greater coverage than today, as that band was less subject to line-of-sight limitations.Â  On the other hand, we know that the old band would have probably suffered more ducting and E-layer skip interference.Â  Anyone who is familiar with the amateur radio 6-meter band at 50 MHz knows that there would have been significant interference whenever skip was “in”. Â FM broadcasting is also better off today because the band change increased FM’s available spectrum almost threefold, giving us 100 channels instead of the original 40.Â  As was already being detected in the early 1940’s, FM’s growth would have eventually been stifled by a lack of frequencies. Â Assigned to a spectrum sandwiched between military frequencies and the low VHF TV channels, there would have been no room for FM broadcasting to expand to meet the eventual demand.Â  Today, as the standard AM band becomes increasingly neglected and handicapped in our high-noise digital world, even the present 88-108 MHz band is being stretched beyond its capacity in many parts of the country.

Â The future course of radio broadcasting is uncertain today.Â  The public now has many more options for aural information and entertainment than it did twenty years ago.Â  We now have our choice between analog and digital AM/FM radio, satellite radio, and streaming Internet music fed through our phones and smart speakers – all of which serves to fractionalize the audience into smaller and smaller parts.Â  Nonetheless, it seems likely that FM radio broadcasting will still be a major source of entertainment and information for at least one more generation of listeners.
