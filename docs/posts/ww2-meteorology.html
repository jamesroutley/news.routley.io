WW2 Meteorology

With the Outbreak of the WW2, Ships in the North Atlantic could no longer Report their Positions & Weather and the Atlantic became what is known in the Jargon as a “Data Sparse Area“.  As an aside, the Rate of Attrition of Merchant Ships in Wartime is demonstrated by the size of the British Voluntary Observing Fleet: the 995 British Ships Reporting Weather in 1939 had been reduced to 480 when Peacetime reporting restarted in 1945. The Target number in 1945 for a reasonable Network of Ocean Observations was 1000 British ships.

Even now with the advent of Remote Sensing Systems like Artificial Satellites, the main method of finding out about the Weather over the Oceans is still to persuade Merchant Ships on Passage to Observe & Report the Weather every 6-hrs. Sixty-odd years ago there was nothing else.  Knowledge of the Weather, particularly predicted Weather, is essential to the prosecution of modern Warfare and those Countries who had Weather data during the War classified them Secret.  Other sources of information had to be found.  One method used was Long-Range Aerial Reconnaissance and, in Britain, the bulk of the Task fell to Coastal Command.  The use of Aircraft for Sounding the Atmosphere over a spot from Ground level to their Ceiling began in WW1, mainly as an aid to Artillery Work, and had been routine since 1925; these provided early examples of the Acronym – “THUM” from “Thermal Upper-Air Measurement” and “PRATA” from “Pressure & Temperature Sounding“. (Nowadays we would have used the Letter “S“).  One such Sounding was made at Worcester using Spitfires for many years until well after the War.  In addition to the Barometer, the Met Observer had 2 Main Instruments: the Psychrometer & the Airspeed Indicator. This latter was essential to apply corrections for the effect of Air Friction to the Temperature Readings. The Psychrometer was an Aviation version of the familiar dry & wet bulb set and was mounted on a strut outside a window close to the Observer with water being supplied to the wet bulb by pumping from a Tank inside the Aircraft.

The Atlantic, however, remained the source of most of Britain’s Weather and the Weather Forecasting Ttechniques of that era required the analysis by hand of Charts plotted by hand with as much detailed Weather Data as possible. Professionally made weather observations from an area chosen by the Forecasters were invaluable and the answer was still Meteorological Reconnaissance.

The RAF Bomber & Coastal Commands were particularly dependent on meteorological advice at all times. Bombers needed to take off & land again safely 8 to 10 hours later and their Crews had to be able to get to and from Target areas and locate Targets without getting lost or straying over heavily defended areas.  Coastal Command Aircraft carried out anti-Submarine Patrols & Convoy Protection duties, meaning Flights could last for 12-hours or more. In both cases the, RAF needed to know as much as possible about Cloud cover, visibility & Upper Air Winds at the Home Station, en-Route, and, at the Target destination. Unfortunately, lack of available data, especially detailed Upper Air Data, meant that the Meteorological Office could provide only limited information and Forecasts were often imprecise at best.

At the start of the War, there were no effective Upper Air Charts and only a limited understanding of the Upper Atmosphere. The Meteorological Office had no internal expertise or even a network of Upper Air Stations and there were just 2 Meteorological Flights making twice-daily soundings to 24,000-ft at RAF Mildenhall & Aldergrove.  Radiosonde Stations were located at Penzance & Lerwick using Finnish & French equipment until these Countries were invaded, at which point the Met Office was forced into Developing & Operating its own version.

In addition, Upper Air Analyses & Forecasts were conducted by the Surface Bench. During 1942 it was decided to create an Upper Air Bench at Dunstable and this came into being in January 1943 under the Supervision of Petterssen.  Sverre Petterssen (19th February 1898 – 31st December 1974) was a Norwegian Meteorologist, prominent in the field of Weather Analysis & Forecasting.  Knowledge was still very limited and although irregular Met Reconnaissance Sorties over the Atlantic started in the late Spring of 1941, it was not until 1944 that more Tracks & Twice daily Sorties were introduced.

RAF Tiree was opened in 1941, with 224 Squadron Flying Hudsons on anti-Submarine Patrol & Air-Sea Rescue. In 1942, No.304 (Polish) Squadron brought a dash of colour to the Base. In the early days, conditions were often uncomfortable.  In 1942 No.518 Meteorological Observer Squadron arrived Flying out into the Atlantic to make Weather measurements.  The flat, wide area of The Reef had already been established as a Civilian Airstrip before the RAF arrived.  Flying had started there around 1929, operating from a Grass Airstrip from 1934 to 1940, and the 1st Air Service beginning on July 1936, by Midland & Scottish Airways, having been delayed by a dispute over Rental for the Field.  RAF Tiree was Constructed with Labourers being brought in from Ireland, and several Scottish Jails, to build the Runways.  Quarries were blasted out at Baugh & Balephetrish to provide hardcore for their Construction, with the ruins of the derelict Glassary Seaweed Factory at Middleton, and the old Sone Storehouse on Scarinish Jetty being claimed for the same purpose. 3 Runways were provided, and 10 half-T2 Type Hangars.  Tiree was classified as a restricted area, and RAF Tiree was Officially opened in November 1941.  The Airfield would eventually Accommodate more than 2,000 Aircrew and support Staff.

In order to get the Ddata for the Forecasts, we had to Monitor the Weather across the Atlantic. This was done from our Base at Tiree in Scotland.  We used Halifax Bombers and flew 10-hour Sorties which followed the following pattern.  We would start out Flying at 95 millibars, about 1500ft.  Every 50 Nautical Miles we would take a Series of Readings from the Nose of the Plane. These Readings included the Type and amount of Cloud, the Top & Base of the Cloud, Visibility, Wind Speed, & Temperature.  Every 200-Miles, we would descend to Sea level to Read the Sea Level Pressure.  At Position 14, about 750-miles out, we would Climb to 500-millibars, about 18,000ft.  We would Zig-Zag up to this height, taking Readings along the way.  At the top we would turn back, and take Readings along the Top for 400-miles.  We would then descend, repeating the Zig-Zag, and take Readings every 50-millibars.  These included readings of Ice & Condensation.  The run ended with a descent to Sea Level and a Sea level Pressure reading.  The Readings were then Logged, Coded on a one time code pad, and then Radioed back to Base, where the Wireless Operators Decoded the Readings. We had to Code the Transmissions to stop the Germans intercepting the Data.  An accurate Weather Forecast would benefit them too.

Work was as follows; an Observation every Hour, Temperature, Pressure, the Wind Direction & Speed, Cloud types, height & quantity, the visibility and Weather condition, all this to be Coded and sent by Teleprinter to Met HQ at Bracknell.  A Chart, as you see now on the Television, had to be produced every 3-hours. Mid-afternoon I had to go to the Aircrew Briefing Room, display a Chart and prepare for the Duty Forecaster to give the details of the Weather conditions at Take-off, over the Target and on their return back to Base.

RAF 518 Meteorological Observer Squadron’s Ready Room on Tiree during WW2

Charles Lamb Met Officer at Abingdon had a large Book of Airfield Weather Diagrams made for his ‘91 Group’ Stations through conversations with Pilots and his local Meteorological Staff, who were able to tell him of special characteristics that had a bearing on the formation/dispersal of low cloud/poor visibility at particular Airfields.  This was the forerunner of a large, orange-covered Book familiar to many Outstation Forecasters entitled Aerodrome Weather Diagrams & Characteristics (MO671), which was 1st Published in 1962 by Bracknell HQ to Assist with Flight-briefing of Aircrew.

Night of the Big Wind – 24/25th  March 1944
The fact that the Met Office still had only a very Limited understanding of Upper Air Winds, in particular, how narrow the Bands of Strong Winds could be, was brought into tragically sharp focus on the Night of 24–25th March 1944. An Allied Bomber Stream destined for Berlin which had been Forecast to meet Winds no stronger than 45 mph was torn apart when it encountered Winds in excess of 120 mph resulting in the loss of 72 Bombers.

811 Aircraft were sent to Berlin.  An exceptionally strong Wind caused the Pathfinders to Overshoot the Aiming Point and the Bombing spread outside the Southern Suburbs.  Considerable damage was, however, inflicted, on the Capital.  The Siemens and 73 other Factories were hit.  72 Aircraft were lost, (8.9%) of which it is estimated that nearly 3-Quarters were due to Flak,  the Wind driving many Bombers off their Course over heavily defended areas. Mosquitos carried out a feint attack on Kiel, and Precision Raids on Munster, Duisburg & on Airfields in the Low Countries.  Other Mosquitos made Intruder & Serrate Patrols, destroying 3 Enemy Aircraft & damaging 4 others for the loss of one British Fighter.  A large diversionary sweep was carried out over France but this did appear to distract the Fighters.
