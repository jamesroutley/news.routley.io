Japan implements language proficiency requirements for certain visa applicants

Japan will require certain foreign nationals applying for its most common white-collar work visa to prove language proficiency, including in Japanese, if their work involves the use of language skills. 

Under new guidelines that apply from Wednesday, certain applicants for the “Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services” visa — widely used by interpreters, company workers, and hotel staff — must submit proof of their language ability. 

The new benchmark has been set at the equivalent of the Common European Framework of Reference (CEFR) B2 level. According to the Justice Ministry, a Japanese Language Proficiency Test (JLPT) N2 certificate or a score of 400 or higher on the Business Japanese Proficiency Test (BJT) can be used to prove Japanese proficiency.

The requirement applies to jobs in which language skills are central, such as translation, interpretation and customer-facing roles. Immigration authorities say the move is aimed at preventing cases in which foreign workers obtain visas under one category, but then engage in unrelated or lower-skilled work.

Those already residing in Japan under the visa category will also need to provide evidence of language ability if the content of their job changes or if they switch occupations to one that involves primarily interacting in another language such as Japanese.

The change forms part of a broader government push to tighten immigration compliance for the visa category. 

In March, authorities also introduced a requirement for both dispatching and receiving companies to submit written pledges affirming that foreign workers under the “Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services” category will not engage in activities outside the scope of their visa status.

Several categories of applicants are exempt from needing to provide a Japanese language examination certificate. These include those who can prove that they have graduated from Japanese universities or vocational schools, individuals who have completed compulsory education in Japan, and long-term residents who have lived in the country for 20 years or more.

However, this new requirement may add pressure to an already strained Japanese- language testing system.

Applications for some levels of the JLPT — widely used as proof of Japanese proficiency — have been closing earlier than scheduled due to surging demand.

This exam is typically held twice a year, in July and December, with some regions only holding it once annually. Applications for the December 2025 session reached a record high of 1,049,597, and the exam was held in 82 countries, including Japan.

For this year’s July test session, Japan Educational Exchanges and Services, the organizer of the exam, stopped accepting applications for certain levels more than 10 days ahead of the original deadline due to overwhelming demand.

The “Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services” status is one of the most common residence categories for foreign professionals in Japan. As of the end of 2025, 475,790 people were living in the country under this visa category, making this the second-largest group after permanent residents.
