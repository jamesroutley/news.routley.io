Your Document Needs...

When I open a business document1 there’s certain things that I always want to see in the header. These orientate me to the document’s origin and how I can use it.

I’ll often ask the author to include these if they’re missing. So here’s my blanket request, future collaborator: include these to begin with, for the sake of me and all your other readers!

Title

“What is it?”

Ideally, this is something that makes sense if pulled into another context – for instance, when used as text for a link to the document.

You’ll note the title of this article in the header…and in your browser’s tab bar, or your bookmarks, etc.

“Who is it?”

The “who” may more properly be an editor or compiler – but it should be a responsible party for the document as of a point in time.

John Truscott Reese would say that this should name a single person; I tend to agree unless its’s a very close collaboration. I’d phrase this in terms of identifying a single “responsible party”, plus diligently and generously acknowledging other contributors.

Another of John’s hobbyhorses is that a name should be an unambiguous identifier within context: not “just a given name”, but a unique given+family name, or username-at-company, or e-mail address, etc.

You’ll notice the header of this page says “cceckman.com”: that’s my unique name, according to ICANN.2

Date

“When is it?”

Pick a meaningful date to put this document in history. Maybe that’s an “originally written”, maybe that’s a “last updated,” but something meaningful to the content.

This is not the same as a manually-curated revision history, or an automated history like a changelog or blame. The date(s) in the header should reflect your (human) understanding of when the document matters, not the full history of every typo correction.

For my blog, I manually provide a date of publication, though I may have been drafting for A While beforehand. If there are meaningful updates, I may add an “updated” stanza – for instance, if an article gets a lot of traffic that I want to respond to.

Canonical link

“Where is it?”

In the world of academic-affiliated publication, the canonical identifier is a [Digital Object Identifier]. A company-internal document might have a shortlink (e.g. a golink).3 These kinds of links aren’t necessarily discoverable when you have the document open, so make them explicit.4

On the World Wide Web, this might just be the URL, if it’s a permalink (as on my site); but be mindful of (re)directing to the canonical page.
