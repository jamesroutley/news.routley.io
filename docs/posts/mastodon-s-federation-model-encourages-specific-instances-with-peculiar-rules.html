Mastodon's federation model encourages specific instances with peculiar rules

what makes a social network interesting is the contributions that the users decide to make available to the world. I am of the opinion that, to give the users the most freedom to let their personality shine, the social network itslef should be as boring and generic as possible.

social networks can of course choose to differenciate themselves by some sort of gimmick: LinkedIn is business-oriented, Instagram is photo-oriented, Tumblr is post-oriented and Twitter is short-form-oriented; but I believe they shouldn't go beyond that.

so why isn't Mastododon a good social network?

imagine if the only online profile where you could express yourself was through your old "Ancient Greek Literature Forum" account. that would be quite limiting, wouldn't it? you'd have to follow the forum rules, which are quite strict and perfectly adequate to regulate discourse about ancient literature, yet completely limiting when wanting to discuss about general topics. you created that account because you wanted to take part on those specific conversations, not because you wanted it to represent your whole online persona.

I feel this is what Mastodon encourages with its federation model. admins create these highly specific instances with peculiar rules, and people who want to create a profile there have to submit themselves to a highly limited set of conversations and content they can publish. what if I feel strongly about some geopolitical topic yet those kind of conversations are banned on my "LEGOholics" instance? where will I voice my opinion? on another server? with another account? what's the point then?

some could say: just create your account on a more generalist instance! that's true, but most people don't want to host generic instances. they go through the hassle of setting up their own if it allows them to create some nice little place entirely dedicated to their passions. generalist intances are few and far apart and they, rightfully, attract the most users. but this creates enormous conglomerates which hold too much power and influence on the fediverse and risk creating great damage with every action they take, such as blacklisting some specific instance or defederating alltogether.

the ideal solution would be to distribute all these users between many small and generic instances with no silly domain names or draconic rule sets and have some way to create "forum-like" instances where no accounts can be created but where specific and highly moderated discussions can take place.

I personally don't think this will ever be possible, it can only serve as some nice little fantasy to keep me dreaming for better online spaces.

P.S.

  • "just run your own instance"

I would like Mastodon to be the best federated social network possible, which is why I want to suggest a better way of federating. Telling people to make their own server is a valid suggestion, but its natural conclusion is a peer 2 peer architecture instead of a federated one. If you want a p2p social network, cool, but that's not what Mastodon was optimized to be.

  • "actually having very specialized communities is good"

Mastodon is a social network, not a multi-forum platform. My proposal for a hybrid system is, I think, the best way to get the best out of both worlds: the freedom and individuality of your own personal social media account and the incredible depth of personalized communities akin to the forum era. Unfortunately this means that Mastodon would have to take some advice both from the Reddit and Facebook models, instead of being a copy of Twitter.
