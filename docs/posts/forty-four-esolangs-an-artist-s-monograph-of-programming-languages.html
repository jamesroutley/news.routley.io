Forty-Four Esolangs: an artist's monograph of programming languages

I have two pieces of esolang news: one personal, the other about this blog.

First, a collection of my esolang work is now out with MIT Press. A sampling of the languages are posted on my site, but that list leans toward the implemented, which excludes some of the more poetic, less implementable langs. The book, like this blog, celebrates playing at the edges of language and code, with some results far from our physical machines. It was a blast to write and forced me to work through on my whole to-do list of esolang ideas: which still resonated for me, and how they might become fully realized. Do I really want to define a language that entirely escapes the act of articulation? (Yes, of course I do!) You can read the preface here. There's more in this piece for IEEE Spectrum and this podcast with Sam Arbesman, author of the brilliant Magic of Code.

Second, it's been quiet here the last few years as I focused on the book. That silence was broken with the excitement of sharing the original, long-lost INTERCAL compiler code, written in SPITBOL and found in Don Woods's garage.
