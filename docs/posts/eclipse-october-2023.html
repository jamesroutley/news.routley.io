Eclipse October 2023

On October 14th, 2023, an annular eclipse passed over a wide swath of the United States, from the Pacific Northwest through Texas.

Grid operators regularly prepare for the unexpected, but perhaps more rare are the predictable events of such a magnitude as to require substantial preparation.

This page displays the data on Grid Status related to the operations during the course of the October 14th eclipse using our custom dashboard features currently under development.

Other generation sources stepping in

An eclipse is a rare but predictable event with real implications for regular operations. To respond to the drop in solar, other generation sources will step in. In a grid with increasing penetration of renewable resources, operators will be required to plan around new events, both predictable and not. A well planned and executed response to a predictable eclipse bodes well for a future where a particularly cloudy day, blizzard conditions across a large area, or widespread hail damage to solar farms could reduce generation by similar MW values.

One facet of the predictable nature of this event is that day-ahead and real-time prices should not experience a wide spread due to the extended time available for ISO operational planning. This is true despite the eclipse's potential to cause one of the largest ramp events in the history of either market, which often are associated with high real-time prices. However, if something unexpected does occur, it should be reflected in the real-time prices seen below.

In preparation for the extreme ramp conditions of the eclipse, ERCOT had a valuable tool at its disposal: the ERCOT Contingency Reserve Service (ECRS). Initially designed to handle net load ramps often caused by solar resources, ECRS was available for deployment. Despite these steep net load ramp conditions, ERCOT ultimately did not need to rely on ECRS. This suggests that ERCOT's plans to "pre-posture the system as necessary to meet both the down and up solar ramps," and the response from market participants, were sufficient.

While an eclipse is entirely a natural phenomenon, the Simpsons has taught us that it may be possible for equally extreme ramps to be introduced by nefarious players, as Mr. Burns showed us.
