I worked at LastPass as an engineer. My 2 cents on the situation

I worked at LastPass as an engineer a long time ago. 7+ years ago. My 2 cents on the situation. This is the worst breach LastPass has had. By a lot. The key difference is that customer vaults were accessed this time, which are kept in a completely separate database. 1/x

LastPass master passwords are used to derive your master key and encrypt your vault entries. There are a few important cryptographic details to note: The key derivation func has changed over time. It's pbkdf2, but the default number of rounds has slowly increased over time

If you had a legacy 5000 rounds or 10000 rounds, the # of rounds can't just be increased server side. Changing that would mean a new key is derived. So older users likely still have a pretty low number of pbkdf2 rounds which makes them more vulnerable to brute forcing

Lots of vault entries may be encrypted with ECB mode AES-256. I worked on supporting unauthenticated CBC mode. But re-encrypting a users vault entries requires having their master key. Legacy entries allow an attacker to see reused passwords in your vault instantly.

URLs aren't encrypted. This has been a well reported criticism of the product. But it allows an attacker to see what vault entries are associated with which sites.

Overall. I think OG users of LP are at greater risk for targeted attacks than newer. I don't think we'll see widespread vaults being cracked, but targeted attacks are very possible with a user's entire vault 6/6

People are also asking if the "Notes" field is encrypted. Secure notes are encrypted, and the notes field are encrypted. I just double checked. The notes field in your vault is the "extra" encrypted blob
