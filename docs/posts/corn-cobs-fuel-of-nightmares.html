So, how do you convey a fluffy, fibrous mash of plant matter into a pyrolyzer‚Ä¶ reliably?

Our biomass processing system grinds a bale of agricultural residue into tiny particles across several steps. Smaller particles have a higher surface area:volume ratio to enable faster heating rates in the pyrolysis reactor, which improves bio-oil yield.

The first step uses a tub grinder to turn a bale into a stream of biomass pieces under 6 inches long. These ‚Äú6-inch-minus‚Äù pieces are then transported to a hammer mill that reduces the pieces to sub-inch particles, capable of being carried by an auger without wrapping around the shaft.

Tub grinder (red, left), pneumatic duct (silver, center) and hammermill (white, right).

And while the tub grinder itself was working fine, the denser corn stover material was no longer able to be transported into the hammermill, causing build-ups at the bottom of the tub grinder and in the outlet structure. Light and flexible pieces of wheat straw in San Francisco had easily been conveyed pneumatically from the tub grinder into the hammer mill. But now we were working with Kansan corn stover with excellent, dense, carbon-rich corn cobs.

And that density was a problem. Even after being processed through the cutting head of the tub grinder, the fragments were too heavy to be reliably conveyed pneumatically. Where an occasional rock (or even handgun!) might gravity-separate and sit idle in an unencumbered biomass stream until the next maintenance window, the large volume of cobs caused blockages throughout the ducting.

With the bottleneck clearly isolated, we decoupled the two systems: material handling and bio-oil production. To keep testing our 10tpd prototype while we solved the corn cob blockage, we added a new side-channel feeding mechanism for biomass pellets. This allowed us to continue pyrolyzing a variety of feedstocks (anything pre-pelletized) while we engineered a new biomass processing solution.

The right way to convey biomass depends on its form factor. Bale transport is different from moving 6‚Äù pieces, which is different from moving biomass dust, etc. Our bottleneck occurred at the 6‚Äù transport stage, and we evaluated three types of conveyance to solve it: pneumatics, augers, and belts.

Our previous design used pneumatic conveyance, but the tub grinder‚Äôs cutting head, in tandem with the hammermill‚Äôs vacuum system, wasn‚Äôt generating enough air flow. Increasing the pressure differential across the problematic conveyance system would be difficult without major changes to the hammermill and downstream hardware. Reducing the piping diameter would have increased the flow velocity, but at the expense of increased risk of blockages and a reduction in maximum material flow. If we introduced yet another source of positive pressure, we‚Äôd have competing sources of air flow (and we‚Äôd likely have to redesign the tub grinder itself). There was no simple fix.

We do use screw augers throughout the system, but here it wasn‚Äôt the right fit here. Long biomass strands and hard chunks can easily strangle a shaft or jam a flight. To convey these cobs with you would need an incredibly large auger with a beefy motor to boot. The geometry in this particular spot was also tough. It‚Äôs challenging to convey biomass with augers, as we‚Äôve written about before!

So... belts! A belt conveyor with cleats (better lifting) and ducting (dust reduction and loose material containment) solved for the geometry, solved for air flow, and is commonly used in similar applications. We had initially not found any easy commercial options for these belts, ranking them lower in the design trade. But with some deeper searching, we found a great, off-the-shelf belt that would arrive quickly. This was an easy choice for the next iteration.
